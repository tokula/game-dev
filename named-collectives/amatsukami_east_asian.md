# Amatsukami

**Native name / Etymology:** 天津神 (Amatsukami) — "Kami of Heaven" or "Heavenly Gods"; from 天 (ama, "heaven/sky") + 津 (tsu, possessive genitive particle) + 神 (kami, "god/spirit/divine being"). Also rendered 天つ神 in older orthography.

**Source Layer:** East Asian

**Primary origin:** Japanese Shinto mythology — Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE); formally codified in Engishiki norito (927 CE)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Named Collective

**Membership:** Open category of all kami dwelling in or born of Takamagahara (天原, the Plain of Heaven). The earliest named subset — the kotoamatsukami (別天津神, "Distinguishing Heavenly Kami") — numbers exactly five, spontaneously generated at creation. The subsequent Seven Divine Generations (神世七代, Kamiyo Nanayo) add twelve more, culminating in the creative pair Izanagi and Izanami. Their offspring (Amaterasu, Tsukuyomi, Susanoo) and later descendants (Ninigi) extend the collective further. Total membership is open-ended; the Engishiki registers 2,861 named shrine kami across Japan, many of Amatsukami lineage.

**Gender(s):** Varied — see individual roster entries

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants, Warrior culture

## One-line lore summary

The divine assembly of Takamagahara — heaven's kami who govern the cosmic order and claim sovereign authority over the earthly realm, ultimately delegating it to the Imperial House through Ninigi's descent.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Celestial mandate — the Amatsukami assert supreme authority over earthly governance; this mandate culminates in the tenson kōrin (天孫降臨, Heavenly Grandchild's Descent), when Ninigi is dispatched to rule the Japanese islands on behalf of Amaterasu and Takamimusubi
- `[major]` Musubi (産巣日, creative generative force) — the musubi deities (Takamimusubi, Kamimusubi) embody the primal force of growth, creation, and life-completion; Kamimusubi revived Ōkuninushi when he was slain by the Yasobito
- `[minor]` Spontaneous generation (hitorigami emergence) — the five kotoamatsukami arose without parents, partners, or cause, then withdrew into concealment; a mode of existence unique among all kami

## Strengths

- Cosmic authority recognized across the kami world; even the ruling Kunitsukami ultimately yield in the kuni-yuzuri (land cession)
- Deliberative governance: major decisions are made by kami council in Takamagahara (convened in the Ame-no-Yasukawara riverbed), lending collective legitimacy to decrees
- Access to sacred regalia and celestial resources: the Three Sacred Treasures (mirror, jewel, sword) originate in Takamagahara

## Characteristics

- Defined by heavenly origin and association with Takamagahara, not by shared physical form; members range from formless cosmic principles to fully embodied, narratively active deities
- Encompass both hitorigami (single, genderless, self-generating kami) and paired creative forces (meoto, husband-wife pairs)
- Many withdraw into concealment (kakuri mashimashi) after fulfilling their cosmic function, becoming inaccessible to later myth
- The distinction between Amatsukami and Kunitsukami was a formal legal-religious category in Heian court ritual; modern Shinto no longer enforces it

## Faults / Weaknesses

- The five kotoamatsukami are hidden and non-interactive after their emergence — their authority cannot be directly petitioned by later kami or mortals
- Internal conflict has destabilized Takamagahara more than once: Susanoo's destructive rampage caused Amaterasu to retreat into Ama-no-Iwato cave, plunging the world into darkness; Tsukuyomi's killing of Ukemochi led to the permanent separation of day and night
- Amaterasu's cave withdrawal demonstrates that a single Amatsukami's emotional state can destabilize the entire cosmos

## Modifiers

- Mandate to create the earthly realm — the kotoamatsukami commanded Izanagi and Izanami to solidify the drifting land with the jewelled spear Ame-no-Nuboko — *divine decree from the kotoamatsukami at creation*

> **Note on sources:** The Kojiki and Nihon Shoki present variant orderings and memberships for the kotoamatsukami and the Kamiyo Nanayo. The Nihon Shoki offers multiple "one book says…" (一書曰) alternatives alongside its main text. The Kojiki is generally treated as the canonical Shinto account. Where the two chronicles disagree (e.g. the Tsukuyomi–Ukemochi killing appears only in the Nihon Shoki; the Kojiki's parallel food-deity killing involves Susanoo instead), both variants are noted in the roster's Individual distinctions.
