# Olympians — Greek Roster

> See also: [olympian_greek.md](olympian_greek.md)

## Command hierarchy

Zeus is king of the Olympians and holds supreme authority on Olympus; Hera is queen as his wife and sister. Below them, no formal rank order among peers is attested in primary sources — Olympian authority is domain-specific rather than hierarchical. Zeus arbitrates disputes between Olympians, though his rulings are sometimes contested or circumvented, as attested throughout the *Iliad*.

**Superiors above the Olympians:** The Moirai (Fates) operate above all Olympians including Zeus; even Zeus defers to or enacts fate rather than overriding it (*Iliad* Bk 16.431–461).

**Source priority:** Primary sources (Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey*; Hesiod's *Theogony*; the *Homeric Hymns*; Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca*) are authoritative.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

**Aphrodite**
- `[Characteristic]` Dual origin — two distinct traditions in primary sources with no ancient reconciliation: *Theogony* (ll. 188–200) gives her sea-foam birth from Uranus's severed genitals, making her older than Zeus; *Iliad* (Bk 5.370–371) names her daughter of Zeus and Dione. Plato's *Symposium* (c. 385–370 BCE, 180d–181b) formalises the divide by treating them as two entirely separate goddesses — Aphrodite Ourania (Heavenly, the sea-foam birth) and Aphrodite Pandemos (Common, daughter of Zeus and Dione) — rather than harmonising them. No other ancient source reconciles the two accounts.
- `[Fault]` Wounded by the mortal Diomedes with Athena's assistance during the Trojan War (*Iliad* Bk 5.334–340); her domain does not extend to direct combat

**Apollo**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Divine archer: his arrows bring plague at distance without physical contact — sent plague on the Greek camp in *Iliad* Bk 1 (ll. 43–52)
- `[Specialization-minor]` Prophetic oracle: the oracle at Delphi speaks his will; no other Olympian has a comparably institutionalised prophetic function in primary sources

**Ares**
- `[Fault]` Wounded twice by the mortal Diomedes in a single battle in *Iliad* Bk 5 — once with Athena's aid; Ares retreats to Olympus each time
- `[Characteristic]` Represents undirected bloodlust rather than strategic war; sided with Troy in the *Iliad* but is consistently ineffectual against the strategy of Athena

**Athena**
- `[Specialization-major]` Born fully armoured directly from Zeus's head after he swallowed her mother Metis — she has no mother and is in some sense an extension of Zeus's own divine intelligence (*Theogony* ll. 924–926)
- `[Characteristic]` Perpetual virgin; her power over warfare is strategic (mētis, cunning) rather than physical

**Demeter**
- `[Specialization-signature]` Sole control over the earth's fertility: when Demeter withdrew in grief for Persephone, crops failed and all life on earth began to die — no other Olympian could restore this; Zeus was ultimately forced to negotiate Persephone's partial return (*Homeric Hymn to Demeter*, ll. 302–313)

**Hades**
- `[Characteristic]` Rules the Underworld as one of the three sovereign brothers; received it by lot after the Titanomachy (*Bibliotheca*, Apollodorus 1.2.1)
- `[Characteristic]` Not counted among the twelve Olympians in primary sources; his permanent domain is below the earth, not on Olympus

**Hephaestus**
- `[Fault]` Lame — attested across sources, but the cause varies by account (see Modifier below)
- `[Modifier]` Three unreconciled accounts across primary sources — the *Iliad* is itself internally contradictory:
  - *Iliad* Bk 1.590–594: Zeus flung him from Olympus when he intervened to protect Hera from Zeus's anger; he landed on Lemnos
  - *Iliad* Bk 18.395–405: Hera threw him because he was lame from birth; he was rescued by the sea-nymph Thetis
  - *Theogony* ll. 927–928: Hera bore him parthenogenetically — without Zeus — in retaliation for Zeus having fathered Athena alone; the lameness/expulsion connection is implied but not stated explicitly
  Apollodorus (*Bibliotheca*) combines elements of the Homeric accounts without resolving the contradiction. No ancient source harmonises all three.

**Hermes**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Psychopomp: guides the shades of the dead to the underworld entrance; uniquely permitted to travel freely between the divine, mortal, and chthonic realms (*Odyssey* Bk 24)
- `[Characteristic]` Divine messenger: the primary emissary between Olympians and the mortal world, and between Olympians themselves

**Hestia**
- `[Characteristic]` No myths of intervention in mortal affairs are attested in primary sources; her role is entirely domestic and sacrificial — the first and last portion of every sacrifice was offered to her (*Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite*)
- `[Characteristic]` Perpetual virgin by choice; one of three virgin Olympians alongside Athena and Artemis

**Poseidon**
- `[Specialization-major]` His emotional state directly governs the sea — his anger produces catastrophic storms, his calm brings fair seas; this physical identity with a natural force is more consistently described in primary sources than that of any other Olympian (*Odyssey* passim)
- `[Characteristic]` Resents Zeus's claim to supreme authority; the *Iliad* shows him requiring Zeus to remind him of their equal standing by the lot-draw that separated their realms (*Iliad* Bk 15.185–199)

**Zeus**
- `[Specialization-signature]` Supreme arbiter of divine order: Zeus guarantees oaths (especially by the Styx), xenia (hospitality laws), and cosmic justice; his assembly authority is unmatched even when contested by other Olympians
- `[Fault]` Bound by the Moirai despite supreme power; in *Iliad* Bk 16 he is explicitly prevented from saving his son Sarpedon from fated death, and Hera warns him the other gods would resent breaking fate
