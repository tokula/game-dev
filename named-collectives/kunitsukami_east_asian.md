# Kunitsukami

**Native name / Etymology:** 国津神 (Kunitsukami) — "Kami of the Land" or "Earthly Gods"; from 国 (kuni, "land/country/realm") + 津 (tsu, possessive genitive particle) + 神 (kami, "god/spirit/divine being"). Also rendered 国つ神 in older orthography.

**Source Layer:** East Asian

**Primary origin:** Japanese Shinto mythology — Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE); formally codified as a ritual-legal category in the Engishiki norito (927 CE)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Named Collective

**Membership:** Open category of indigenous kami who dwell in or arose from Ashihara no Nakatsukuni (葦原中国, the Central Land of Reed Plains — the manifest mortal world). Membership is defined by earthly origin and dwelling, contrasted with the heavenly Amatsukami. Principal named members in the kuni-yuzuri (国譲り, land-cession) narrative are Ōkuninushi, Sukunabikona, Kotoshironushi, and Takeminakata; the broader category encompasses the Yasobito (Eighty Kami) and countless unnamed regional tutelary kami. Modern Shinto no longer formally enforces the Amatsukami / Kunitsukami distinction.

**Gender(s):** Varied — see individual roster entries

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants, Nomadic

## One-line lore summary

The indigenous divine rulers of the earthly realm — builders of land, medicine, and civilization — who ceded sovereignty to the heavenly Amatsukami in the kuni-yuzuri myth, retaining their cultic role as regional tutelary kami.

## Specializations

- `[major]` Civilization building — Ōkuninushi and Sukunabikona jointly developed agriculture, medicine, and sericulture across the Japanese islands, establishing the foundations of human flourishing before the heavenly descent
- `[minor]` Territorial dominion — principal Kunitsukami hold governing authority over specific geographic regions and their inhabitants as tutelary deities (ujigami)

## Strengths

- Intimate knowledge of the earthly realm and its peoples, built through direct long-term stewardship
- Tutelary bonds with human communities; venerated as local patron kami at regional shrines
- Coalition-building capacity: Ōkuninushi assembled many kami allies during his rule
- Sukunabikona's specialized knowledge of medicine and grain cultivation complemented Ōkuninushi's territorial authority

## Characteristics

- Defined by earthly dwelling and indigenous origin rather than shared physical form; membership ranges from the supreme ruler Ōkuninushi to unnamed local field kami
- Tutelary in nature: bound to specific territories and their inhabitants
- Operate under Ōkuninushi's leadership in the major Kojiki and Nihon Shoki narratives of the kuni-yuzuri
- Retained cult veneration within specific shrines and regions even after ceding political sovereignty to the Amatsukami
- The distinction between Amatsukami and Kunitsukami is a formal Heian-period ritual-legal category; it was not consistently maintained across all primary sources

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Subordinate in cosmic hierarchy to the Amatsukami; earthly sovereignty ultimately ceded under coercion
- The Yasobito (the Eighty Kami, brothers of Ōkuninushi) represent an unstable, self-interested faction: they murdered Ōkuninushi twice before he came to power
- Takeminakata's military resistance to the Amatsukami's emissary Takemikazuchi ended in defeat; Kunitsukami force was insufficient to hold sovereignty against heavenly power

## Modifiers

- Compelled to cede earthly sovereignty in the kuni-yuzuri — *divine demand issued by Amaterasu and Takamimusubi via successive emissary missions*; compliance secured by Ōkuninushi in exchange for construction of a grand palace shrine (Izumo Taisha) and continued ritual veneration

---

> **Note on sources:** The Kojiki presents Ōkuninushi under multiple names and aspects — Ōnamuchi-no-Kami (大穴牟遅神), Ashihara-no-Shikoo (葦原色許男), Utsushikome-no-Kami (宇都志国玉神), and others. Shinto exegesis treats these as epithets of one deity, but the Kojiki does not explicitly equate all of them; they may have been distinct regional kami syncretized over time. The Nihon Shoki offers variant accounts of the kuni-yuzuri, including different emissaries dispatched by the Amatsukami and different responses from the Kunitsukami.
