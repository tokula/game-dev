# Kunitsukami — East Asian Roster

> See also: [kunitsukami_east_asian.md](kunitsukami_east_asian.md)

## Command hierarchy

Ōkuninushi-no-Mikoto holds supreme authority over the Kunitsukami as the ruler of Ashihara no Nakatsukuni at the time of the kuni-yuzuri. Sukunabikona served as his co-equal partner in the civilization-building era but departed before the land cession. Kotoshironushi and Takeminakata are Ōkuninushi's sons and act as subordinate lords whose consent or resistance determines the outcome of the kuni-yuzuri. The Yasobito (Eighty Kami) are Ōkuninushi's brothers — originally rivals, later subordinates.

```
Ōkuninushi-no-Mikoto — supreme ruler of the earthly realm (Ashihara no Nakatsukuni)
  ├─ Sukunabikona-no-Kami — co-ruler (partner); departed to Tokoyo no Kuni
  ├─ Kotoshironushi-no-Kami — son; consented to kuni-yuzuri
  ├─ Takeminakata-no-Kami — son; resisted; confined to Suwa Lake
  └─ Yasobito (Eighty Kami) — brothers; original rivals; subordinate after Ōkuninushi's rise
```

**Source priority:** Primary sources (Kojiki, Nihon Shoki, Engishiki) are the authoritative origin. All entries below derive from these texts. Derivative sections exist for cross-reference only.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

**Kotoshironushi-no-Kami**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Oracular authority — functions as a divine spokesperson (kotoshiro, "one who speaks the word of affairs"); his consent carries the weight of divine decree in the kuni-yuzuri negotiations
- `[Modifier]` Ritual withdrawal — after consenting to the land cession, Kotoshironushi stamped the boat beneath him, covered his face with his sleeve, and slipped into the sea — *self-imposed ritual withdrawal following the kuni-yuzuri*; interpreted in Shinto exegesis as the withdrawal of the earthly divine authority beneath the waves
- `[Characteristic]` Patron of fishermen and diviners; enshrined at Miho no Matsushima and associated with abundant fishing

**Ōkuninushi-no-Mikoto**
- `[Specialization-signature]` Civilization-founding — jointly with Sukunabikona, developed agriculture, medicine (including remedies for illness), sericulture, and practical knowledge for human flourishing across the Japanese islands; the scope of this work establishes him as the principal culture-hero of the Kunitsukami
- `[Specialization-major]` Earthly sovereignty — ruled all of Ashihara no Nakatsukuni as its supreme lord; held authority over the fate of humans (marriage, matchmaking) at Izumo
- `[Modifier]` Revived twice — murdered by his brothers (first by a boulder, then by fire); revived on each occasion through divine intervention: Kamimusubi (or her agents) nursed him back to life — *consequence of the Yasobito's jealousy over his acquisition of Princess Yagami-hime*
- `[Modifier]` Ceded earthly sovereignty to Ninigi in exchange for a grand cultic palace — *divine covenant with Amaterasu and Takamimusubi, brokered after Takemikazuchi's final emissary mission*; Ōkuninushi withdrew to govern the "hidden world" (kakuriyo) of spirits and fate while the visible world (utsushiyo) passed to the imperial line
- `[Characteristic]` Known by multiple epithets in the Kojiki — Ōnamuchi-no-Kami, Ashihara-no-Shikoo, Utsushikome-no-Kami, Ōkunitama-no-Kami — reflecting likely syncretism of originally distinct regional deities under a single narrative figure

> **Note:** The scholarly consensus is that Ōkuninushi's multiple names reflect the gradual syncretism of originally distinct regional kami under a single narrative figure. The Nihon Shoki gives seven epithets (including Ōmononushi and Kunitsukuri Ōanamuchi); the Kojiki gives five. The provincial fudoki gazetteers (8th century CE, PD) preserve additional names used for locally venerated deities later equated with Ōkuninushi: the Izumo Fudoki names him "Ōanamochi-no-Mikoto," the Harima Fudoki uses "Ōnamuchi-no-Mikoto," and also identifies "Iwa-no-Ōkami" (Great Deity of Iwa) with the same figure. The Nihon Shoki explicitly describes Ōmononushi as Ōkuninushi's *sakimitama* (harmonious spirit) and *kushimitama* (wondrous spirit) — treating it as an aspect or emanation rather than a separate deity, but this equation itself may be a harmonizing editorial choice. The Kojiki does not make this identification explicit. See the profile's Sources note.

**Sukunabikona-no-Kami**
- `[Specialization-major]` Diminutive form — described as arriving in a tiny boat made from a millet stalk, dressed in moth-wing garments; too small to be held in the palm; his small scale is a defined physical attribute, not a metaphor
- `[Specialization-major]` Medical and agricultural knowledge — his contribution to the civilization-building partnership covers medicine (including cures for illness and charms against insects), alcohol brewing, and grain cultivation; this knowledge is his primary domain, complementing Ōkuninushi's territorial authority
- `[Characteristic]` Voluntary departure — upon completing his work with Ōkuninushi, Sukunabikona bounced on a millet stalk and departed to the Tokoyo no Kuni (the Eternal Land beyond the sea); his departure prompted Ōkuninushi's declaration that the world-building was incomplete without him, until a divine light from the sea (later identified as Ōmononushi) appeared to complete the task

**Takeminakata-no-Kami**
- `[Specialization-major]` Martial force — challenged Takemikazuchi (Amatsukami's warrior emissary) to a strength contest; grasped Takemikazuchi's hand and was countered when Takemikazuchi transformed his arm into ice and then a sword blade
- `[Fault]` Defeated by Takemikazuchi — despite great physical strength, Takeminakata was overcome; he fled across the sea to Suwa (modern Nagano Prefecture)
- `[Modifier]` Oath of confinement — at Suwa Lake, Takeminakata swore never to leave the region — *oath sworn as a condition of Takemikazuchi halting pursuit*; he remains confined to Suwa as the enshrined deity of Suwa Taisha
- `[Characteristic]` Patron of hunting, martial arts, and sumo wrestling; enshrined at Suwa Taisha (upper and lower shrines, Nagano); one of the most widely venerated warrior kami outside Izumo

**Yasobito (the Eighty Kami)**
- `[Fault]` Collective antagonism — motivated by jealousy over Ōkuninushi's divine favour and marriages, the Eighty Kami murdered him twice using deception (rolling a heated boulder down a slope; burning a trapped boar that was actually fire); both times Ōkuninushi was revived by divine intervention
- `[Characteristic]` Later subordinated — after Ōkuninushi rose to power, the Yasobito became subordinate to him; they function in the narrative primarily as obstacles demonstrating Ōkuninushi's capacity for repeated resurrection and ultimate triumph rather than as individually distinct characters
