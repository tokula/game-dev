# Three Sovereigns

**Native name / Etymology:** 三皇 (Sān Huáng — "Three Sovereigns" or "Three August Ones"; *sān* 三 "three" + *huáng* 皇 "sovereign, august ruler, emperor"; the character 皇 combines 白 "white/bright" over 王 "king", connoting the radiance of supreme authority)

**Source Layer:** East Asian

**Primary origin:** Ancient Chinese mythology; the concept is attested in Han dynasty classics (c. 2nd century BCE – 2nd century CE) though rooted in earlier oral tradition; individual figures (Fuxi, Nüwa) are named as early as the Chuci (c. 3rd–2nd century BCE) and Liezi (pre-Han material, compiled c. 3rd century CE); the group as a named triad is a Han-era systematization of older, separate traditions

**Biological Form:** Varied — Fuxi and Nüwa are depicted with human upper bodies and serpent or dragon lower bodies in Han dynasty art and literature; Shennong has a human body and bull/ox head (人身牛首) per Shanhaijing; the abstract "Heavenly, Earthly, Human Sovereigns" variant has no attested physical form

**Entity type:** Named Collective

**Membership:** 3 — fixed in number but variable in identity across sources; five distinct membership lists are attested in Han dynasty classics; the two most prominent are Fuxi–Nüwa–Shennong (popular tradition) and Fuxi–Shennong–Huangdi (post-Han dominant per Shangshu promotion); see Sources note below

**Gender(s):** Varied — see individual roster entries (Fuxi male; Nüwa female; Shennong male in canonical sources)

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants, Scholarly

## One-line lore summary

Divine creator-sovereigns of the pre-dynastic mythological age who shaped the world, brought forth humanity, and bestowed the first gifts of civilization — writing, medicine, agriculture, and the mending of the sky itself.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` World-shaping creative acts — the San Huang are credited with permanent, unrepeatable alterations to existence: Nüwa created humanity from clay and repaired the shattered sky; Fuxi structured nature through the Eight Trigrams (八卦); Shennong established the principles of medicine by personally testing every herb. No subsequent race, deity, or mortal is attributed creative acts of this scope.
- `[major]` Foundational civilizational gift — each Sovereign delivered a singular domain of knowledge or technique (writing/divination, agriculture/medicine, fire/trapping) as a one-time sovereign act; these gifts define what it means for human civilization to exist

## Strengths

- Divine lineage granting access to powers beyond the reach of mortal cultivation or training
- Each Sovereign holds near-perfect mastery in their respective domain — a depth that mortals can emulate but never equal from first principles

## Characteristics

- Pre-dynastic placement — the San Huang occupy mythological rather than historical time; they precede the Five Emperors and all recorded dynasties
- Hybrid or non-human physical form — serpent lower bodies (Fuxi, Nüwa) and ox/bull head (Shennong) mark them as clearly beyond the human; the abstract "Heavenly, Earthly, Human Sovereigns" variant removes individual identity entirely
- Creation as defining role — each Sovereign is identified not by rule or governance but by a singular creative or founding act; they are what they made or gave
- Variable collective identity — because different classical texts enumerate different members, the San Huang function as a structural concept (the age of divine founders) as much as a fixed group of persons

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Cosmic vulnerability — the era of the San Huang was not stable; the sky broke, floods came; divine nature did not grant immunity to catastrophic events, only the power to respond to them
- Non-self-sustaining gifts — each Sovereign gives their gift once; there is no ongoing provision or repair from the San Huang themselves; humanity inherits the gift and must sustain it independently

## Modifiers

None attested for the group as a whole in primary sources. Individual figures carry specific divine roles or compulsions — see Individual distinctions in the roster.

---

> **Note on membership:** The canonical composition of the Three Sovereigns is not fixed. Five distinct membership lists appear in Han dynasty classics:
> 1. Suiren 燧人, Fuxi 伏羲, Shennong 神農
> 2. **Fuxi 伏羲, Nüwa 女娲, Shennong 神農** — dominant in popular tradition
> 3. Fuxi 伏羲, Zhurong 祝融, Shennong 神農
> 4. Fuxi 伏羲, Shennong 神農, Gonggong 共工
> 5. **Fuxi 伏羲, Shennong 神農, Huangdi 黃帝** — promoted by the Shangshu (Book of Documents); widely cited post-Han
>
> A sixth framework identifies the Three Sovereigns as the abstract categories **Tiān Huáng** (天皇, Heavenly Sovereign), **Dì Huáng** (地皇, Earthly Sovereign), and **Rén Huáng** (人皇, Human Sovereign) — cosmic categories rather than named individuals.
>
> This profile uses the **Fuxi, Nüwa, Shennong** list as primary (most common in modern reference and popular tradition) and notes the Fuxi, Shennong, Huangdi list as the leading alternative.
>
> **Huangdi overlap:** In list #5, Huangdi (Yellow Emperor) appears as a Three Sovereign. He also leads the Five Emperors in *Shiji*. He should be understood as belonging to both traditions depending on the source. See [wu_di_east_asian.md](wu_di_east_asian.md).

> **Note on Shiji:** The "Supplementary Annals of the Three Sovereigns" (補三皇本紀) in the *Shiji* was not written by Sima Qian. It was added by Tang dynasty scholar Sima Zhen (司馬貞) in the 8th century CE. Sima Qian's original *Shiji* begins directly with the Five Emperors. Both texts are PD regardless of their date, but attribution must distinguish the two authors.

### Primary sources

- `[PD]` Chuci 楚辭 (Songs of Chu), various authors, c. 3rd–2nd century BCE — "Tian Wen" (天問, Asking Heaven) chapter by Qu Yuan contains the earliest known written mention of Nüwa by name; describes her creating humans from yellow earth
- `[PD]` Liezi 列子, attr. Lie Yukou; compiled c. 3rd century CE — earliest literary reference to Nüwa repairing the heavens after catastrophic sky-collapse
- `[PD]` Shanhaijing 山海經 (Classic of Mountains and Seas), anonymous, compiled c. 475–100 BCE — includes Shennong's physical description (human body, bull head) and related cosmological figures
- `[PD]` Shiji 史記 (Records of the Grand Historian), Sima Qian c. 91 BCE; Supplementary Annals of the Three Sovereigns (補三皇本紀) added by Sima Zhen (Tang dynasty, 8th century CE) — lists Fuxi, Nüwa, Shennong as the Three Sovereigns
- `[PD]` Huainanzi 淮南子, Liu An et al., c. 139 BCE — cosmological framing for the pre-dynastic era
