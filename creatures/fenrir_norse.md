# Fenrir

**Native name / Etymology:** Fenrir (Old Norse) — etymology uncertain; possibly from *fen* "marsh, bog" + *rir* (a nominal suffix); also called Fenrisúlfr ("Fenris-wolf"), Hróðvitnir ("fame-wolf" or "mighty wolf"), and Vánargandr ("monster of the river Ván," named for the river formed from his saliva while bound)

**Source Layer:** Norse

**Primary origin:** Norse mythology — Prose Edda, Snorri Sturluson (c. 1220) and Poetic Edda (anonymous, c. 10th–13th century)

**Biological Form:** Beastfolk

**Entity type:** Unique creature

**Scale:** Cosmic — at Ragnarök his open jaws span from earth to sky; he swallows Odin, chief of the Æsir; his binding is one of the central acts of cosmic preservation — the gods delayed Ragnarök specifically to keep him contained

**Parent species:** None — unique offspring of Loki and the jötunn Angrboða; sibling to Jörmungandr (the World Serpent) and Hel (ruler of the dead)

**Disposition:** Initially passive — raised among the Æsir as a young wolf; became increasingly threatening as he grew without limit; actively hostile after being tricked into binding; catastrophically destructive at Ragnarök

**Gender(s):** Male — consistently referred to with masculine pronouns in primary sources

## One-line lore summary

The monstrous wolf-child of Loki, bound by divine deception until Ragnarök, when he breaks free to swallow Odin before being slain by the silent god Víðarr.

## Physical description

A wolf of immense size whose growth outpaced the gods' ability to manage him. Only the Dvergar-forged ribbon Gleipnir — not iron chains — was strong enough to hold him.

At Ragnarök he advances with his upper jaw touching the sky and his lower jaw resting on the earth, eyes and nostrils blazing with fire. His howl is described as horrible. While bound, saliva running from his mouth forms the river Ván (Old Norse: "hope").

No specific physical features beyond scale and the blazing eyes and nostrils are attested in the Eddas during his bound period. The spanning-jaw description applies specifically to his Ragnarök manifestation.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **God-devouring** — swallows Odin, the Allfather and chief of the Æsir, at Ragnarök; no other being in Norse mythology kills Odin in the primary sources
- `[major]` **Limitless growth** — grew so rapidly and without apparent ceiling that the Æsir abandoned attempts at physical containment; at Ragnarök his jaws span the full vertical extent of the cosmos

## Strengths

- Physical power sufficient that no chain forged by the Æsir could hold him — only Gleipnir, made from impossible things, succeeded
- Intimidating enough that only Týr among the Æsir was willing to approach and feed him during his early life

## Characteristics

- His binding is maintained by Gleipnir, anchored to a boulder called Gjöll and tethered to the rock Thviti — the restraint is elaborate precisely because his strength demands it
- The river Ván (meaning "hope") takes its name from his saliva while he is bound — an ironic detail given that he represents the termination of the gods' age
- Fated symmetry: he kills Odin; Víðarr kills him — one death balances the other at Ragnarök

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Could be deceived — accepted Týr's hand as a pledge of good faith and allowed himself to be bound by what appeared to be a silken ribbon, not recognising the trap until it was closed
- Killed by Víðarr at Ragnarök, despite slaying Odin — his death is certain even as he achieves his greatest act

## Modifiers

- **Bound by Gleipnir** — the Dvergar-forged magical ribbon made from six impossible ingredients (the sound of a cat's footstep, a woman's beard, mountain roots, bear's sinews, fish's breath, and bird's spittle); smooth as silk but unbreakable; he remains bound on the island Lyngvi until Ragnarök — *Prose Edda (Gylfaginning)*
- **Týr's hand as pledge** — Fenrir demanded that one of the Æsir place a hand in his mouth as a surety of good faith before accepting the fetter; Týr alone agreed and lost his right hand when Fenrir realised he had been deceived — *Prose Edda (Gylfaginning)*

## Ecology

**Prey:** Not documented during the cosmic age while bound; at Ragnarök he swallows Odin — the single attested act of predation in primary sources

**Predators:** Víðarr is his destined killer at Ragnarök; no natural predators are described during the cosmic age

> **Note on sources:** Fenrir's role and fate are consistent across the Poetic and Prose Edda. The minor conflict between sources concerns Víðarr's killing method — *Gylfaginning* describes driving a sword or stepping on Fenrir's jaw; *Völuspá* is less specific. Both agree that Víðarr kills Fenrir immediately after Fenrir swallows Odin.
