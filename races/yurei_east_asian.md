# Yūrei

> See also: [yurei_east_asian_roster.md](yurei_east_asian_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** 幽霊 (yūrei) — 幽 (*yū*, "faint, dim, secluded") + 霊 (*rei*, "soul, spirit"); literally "faint spirit" or "dim soul". Related terms: *bōrei* (亡霊, "departed spirit"), *shiryō* (死霊, "dead spirit"), *onryō* (怨霊, "vengeful spirit" — the most dangerous subtype)

**Source Layer:** East Asian (Japanese)

**Primary origin:** Japanese Shinto and Buddhist death belief; supernatural death narratives documented from *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE); the goryō (noble vengeful spirit) cult attested in *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE); major literary codification in Heian–Kamakura setsuwa collections and Edo period kaidan literature

**Biological Form:** Undead

**Entity type:** Race/Species

> **Note:** Yūrei are a distinct subtype within the broader yōkai category, set apart by their human origin — they are spirits of the human dead rather than supernatural entities that were never human. The primary distinction from other yōkai is purpose-driven behaviour: a yūrei haunts for a specific reason tied to its death. See [yokai_east_asian.md](yokai_east_asian.md) for the parent category.

**Gender(s):** Male and Female — both extensively attested; the three canonical literary yūrei (*San O-Yūrei*) are female, and the dominant visual archetype in Edo art is female; historical onryō (*goryō*) include both male (Prince Nagaya, Prince Sawara) and female figures; no inherent gender restriction exists

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Corrupted/exiled, Nomadic

## One-line lore summary

Spirits of the human dead who cannot pass on — bound to the living world by violent death, failed funeral rites, or overwhelming emotion — who haunt until their purpose is fulfilled or a priest dissolves their tie.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Onryō wrath projection** — the most powerful yūrei (onryō and goryō) can project their rage outward as cascading disasters: epidemics, lightning strikes, fires, drought, and famine afflicting entire cities or provinces, independent of any direct contact with a specific target; no living being could cause equivalent harm (*Shoku Nihongi*, 797 CE; *Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE)
- `[major]` **Spiritual possession** — yūrei can enter and control a living person's body, inducing wasting illness, madness, violent behaviour, or death; onryō possession in particular leaves the host permanently weakened even after exorcism (*Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- `[minor]` **Hitodama manifestation** — accompanied by floating will-o'-the-wisps (*hitodama*) in blue, green, or purple that appear before the spirit fully manifests; the only advance signal of a yūrei's approach (*Ugetsu Monogatari*, 1776)

## Strengths

- Purpose-driven persistence — cannot be deterred by physical force or threats; will continue haunting until their specific purpose is achieved or they are ritually dissolved; immune to exhaustion and physical pain
- Incorporeality — cannot be harmed by physical weapons; pass through walls, doors, and barriers without impediment
- Temporal peak — at the *ushi no koku* (hour of the ox, 2:00–2:30 AM), power and visibility are maximised; during this window they manifest more fully and act with greater force (*Ugetsu Monogatari*, 1776)

## Characteristics

- Canonical appearance (codified through Edo period art and theater): white burial kimono (*katabira*), long black disheveled hair, no visible feet (floating or trailing into void), limp hands dangling from outstretched forearms, *hitodama* flames nearby
- Exclusively human in origin — every yūrei was once a living person; their personality, obsessions, and relationships from life define their haunting pattern
- Purpose-specific rather than random — yūrei are bound to a specific grievance, person, or location; they do not haunt indiscriminately
- Recognised subtypes: **onryō** (vengeful, dangerous, active curse); **goryō** (noble person who died by injustice; capable of city-scale disasters); **ubume** (died in childbirth or leaving children; returns to care for them); **funayūrei** (drowned at sea; sometimes depicted as fish-like humanoids near water); **jibakurei** (earth-bound; cannot leave a specific location); **zashiki-warashi** (child ghost; mischievous rather than dangerous); **fuyūrei** (purposeless, wandering)

## Faults / Weaknesses

- **Ofuda vulnerability** — a holy Shinto slip (*ofuda*) placed on the yūrei's forehead can banish or bind it; the most direct single counter-action (*Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- **Buddhist ritual resolution** — proper funeral rites (*kuyō*) performed by a priest complete the spirit's passage and dissolve it permanently; once properly interred with all rites, the yūrei cannot linger
- **Conditional existence** — a yūrei ceases to exist once its unresolved purpose is completed, its emotional binding is severed, or its body is properly buried; it has no ability to refuse resolution once the condition is met

## Modifiers

- **Binding emotion** — the specific emotion that created the yūrei (revenge, love, grief, jealousy) functions as a compulsion the yūrei cannot escape through willpower alone; external intervention (resolution of the cause, or ritual) is required to break it — *Ugetsu Monogatari* (1776); *Yotsuya Kaidan* (1825)

## Allies / Rivals / Enemies

| Faction | Standing | Notes | Source |
|---------|----------|-------|--------|
| Living humans | Rival | Most yūrei target specific people or places connected to their death; bystanders may be unintentionally harmed, especially by onryō and goryō whose wrath radiates outward without discrimination | *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE); *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |
| Buddhist clergy | Enemy | Priests perform the death rites and exorcisms that pacify or permanently dissolve yūrei; the primary institutional mechanism for ending a haunting | *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE); *Ugetsu Monogatari* (1776) |
| Onmyōji | Enemy | Court yin-yang diviners who detect and banish supernatural entities; particularly active against politically dangerous goryō | *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE); *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE) |
| Kami | Superior | Sufficiently powerful or politically significant onryō may be enshrined and deified as kami to appease them (goryō cult); enshrinement transforms the yūrei into a kami, superseding and ending the haunting | *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE); *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE) |

## Encounter conditions

| Mode | Trigger / Condition | Protocol | Source |
|------|---------------------|----------|--------|
| Hour of the Ox (*ushi no koku*) | The period 2:00–2:30 AM, when the boundary between living and dead is thinnest and yūrei power peaks | Avoid being alone and awake at this hour; extinguish lights; recite Buddhist sutras; direct eye contact risks possession or curse; do not call out names, as a yūrei may answer | *Ugetsu Monogatari* (1776); *Yotsuya Kaidan* (1825) |
| Bound location encounter | Entering a jibakurei's binding site (a well, room, crossroads, or grave); the spirit cannot leave but automatically manifests for any intruder | Do not disturb the binding location or touch the spirit's remains; if it manifests, speak respectfully and acknowledge the grievance; running triggers pursuit instinct in some subtypes | *Banchō Sarayashiki* (c. 1741); *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |
| Hyakumonogatari kaidankai (百物語怪談会) | 100 ghost stories told in sequence by candlelight, extinguishing one candle per story; the ritual thins the boundary with every story told | Do not complete all 100 stories without a priest or onmyōji present; interrupting mid-ritual is equally dangerous; the darkness after the final candle is extinguished is when the spirit enters | *Tonoigusa* (1660) |

## Ecology

**Prey:** No biological prey; onryō and goryō drain life force or induce wasting illness in their specific targets; some accounts describe the suffering of victims as sustaining the yūrei's bond to the living world; non-vengeful subtypes (ubume, zashiki-warashi) do not prey on the living

**Predators:** Buddhist priests (ritual dissolution); onmyōji (detection and banishment); ofuda (binding and banishment); proper funeral rites (structural dissolution); enshrinement as kami (transformation)

---

> **Note on sources:** The visual and behavioral archetype of the yūrei was largely standardised during the Edo period (1603–1868) through kabuki theater and ukiyo-e art. Earlier texts describe the phenomenon of vengeful or restless spirits without the fixed visual conventions. The canonical appearance — white kimono, disheveled black hair, no feet — is a theatrical convention that became the cultural standard; primary sources predating this convention describe yūrei functionally rather than visually. Designers drawing on pre-Edo sources will find more variation in appearance than the modern archetype suggests.
