# Laumė / Lauma

> See also: [lauma_baltic_roster.md](lauma_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Laumė (Lithuanian), Lauma (Latvian), pl. Laumės / Laumas — meaning uncertain; modern Lithuanian *laumė* denotes "mythological being; elf; fairy; pixie"; Latvian *lauma* shifted to mean "witch" under early-modern Christian influence; earlier Latvian sources (Georg Mancelius, *Lettus*, 1638) gloss the word as *fliegende Zauberin* ("flying sorceress"), reflecting an older supernatural stratum; the rainbow is called *Laumės juosta* ("Laumė's sash") in Lithuanian, and *Vaivos juosta* in a named variant after the individual Laumė called Vaiva

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian and Latvian oral tradition — *dainos* (folk songs) and *pasakos / pasakas* (folk tales); Jonas Basanavičius, *Iš gyvenimo vėlių bei velnių* (1903–1904), contains approximately 150 texts mentioning laumės; Krišjānis Barons, *Latvju dainas* (1894–1915, ~3,100 relevant texts), documents the Latvian *lauma* tradition; Georg Mancelius, *Lettus* (1638), provides the earliest written Latvian reference

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Gender(s):** Female — primary attestation across all sources; no male equivalent is named or attested

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants

## One-line lore summary

Lithuanian and Latvian fate-spirits who spin and weave the cloth of human destiny, protect orphans with fierce devotion, and consume men who enter their territory — a class equally compassionate guardian and predatory seductress.

## Specializations

- `[major]` Shapeshifting — attested forms span multiple evolutionary strata of the tradition: archaic bird forms (hen, rooster, pigeon); monstrous hybrid forms (goat-human, mare, bear, dog, snake); fully humanized form (beautiful woman); in the Latvian corpus a soul-release form as a mouse with a silver bell is also attested
- `[minor]` Fate-weaving — visit newborns with distaffs and spindles to spin and weave the cloth of a child's life, assigning or forecasting destiny; Thursday nights are sacred to their spinning; in some folk texts they appear as elderly women at christenings
- `[minor]` Weather manipulation — raise storms, hail, and rain through singing, dancing, and cursing; the rainbow (*Laumės juosta*) is a direct linguistic trace of their atmospheric role; dancing in dew-covered meadows at New Moon and Full Moon is associated with rainfall

## Strengths

- Deep prophetic sight — all Laumės possess at minimum the ability to perceive and declare the fate of a newborn; some see past and future clearly
- Night-active without penalty; darkness and liminal twilight are their native operating conditions
- Motherly competence — exceptional carers for orphans and abandoned children; they take on the role of spiritual foster mothers when no mortal mother survives
- Collective intensity at New Moon and Full Moon — power peaks during these liminal astronomical periods, when they gather in dew-covered meadows and sacred sites
- Expert psychological manipulation of men — seduction operates at a level no ordinary mortal can resist
- Yarn-substitution magic — if deprived of yarn while spinning, can substitute women's hair, entrails, or veins without interrupting the working

## Characteristics

- Not born from mortal humans — an inherent supernatural category; distinct from mortal practitioners of magic (*raganos*), though later sources blur this line
- Physical appearance spans a wide evolutionary range: from archaic bird-beings to monstrous hybrids (stone or bone fingers, sharp stone nails, large teeth) to humanized beautiful women; fossil belemnites found on the ground were called *laumės spenelis* ("Laumė's nipples"), associating the archaic form with large stone-like features
- Dwell near water and in liminal spaces: forests, lakes, swamps, abandoned bathhouses (*pirties*), meadows; many Lithuanian lake names derive from *laumė*
- Solitary in daily life; gather at New Moon and Full Moon in meadows, and on *Laumių diena* (Friday of New Moon being one attested peak day)
- Demand acknowledgment and respect — punish those who ridicule them and those who are lazy; the moral-enforcement drive is intrinsic, not optional
- Cannot have children of their own — their drive toward orphans is connected to this limitation; they may steal or swap babies

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Iron aversion — iron tools serve as effective apotropaic against Laumės; they flee iron
- Spiritually barren — cannot produce their own offspring; this drives both the baby-swapping behavior and their strong pull toward orphans and the motherless
- Vindictive when disrespected or mocked — vengeance is disproportionate and immediate

## Modifiers

- Under Laima's authority — *Laima (the fate goddess) decrees human destiny; the Laumės execute it, literally spinning and weaving the cloth of human life; the relationship is sovereign-to-agent; origin: Lithuanian and Latvian oral tradition*
- Post-Christianization conflation with raganos (witches) — *imposed cultural overlay; especially in Latvian tradition, the lauma was incorporated into the witchcraft belief system during the 16th–18th century witch-persecution period; Lithuanian tradition preserved the older supernatural-type distinction more fully; this conflation is not pre-Christian fact; origin: 16th–18th century missionary and trial records*

## Ecology

**Prey:** Men who enter their territory — seduced and consumed until exhausted, then their bodies eaten; mortals who mock or disrespect them (targeted for curse or direct harm); lazy individuals within their domain (moral-enforcement victims)

**Predators:** Humans wielding iron tools (the only attested reliable counter); Christian religious authority in the post-Christianization period (institutional suppression)

> **Note on sources:** The etymology of *laumė* is contested. The proposed IE root connecting the figure to fertility is one scholarly hypothesis; the near-homophony with *laima* ("luck, fate") has generated significant confusion in secondary literature between Laumės (plural supernatural class) and Laima (singular fate goddess who commands them). The physical description spans an archaic-to-humanized evolutionary spectrum reflecting different strata of folk memory: the archaic bird-being layer is likely oldest, the monstrous hybrid layer intermediate, and the humanized beautiful-woman form latest. Sandis Laime ("Latvian Laumas," *Tautosakos darbai* 62, 2021) documents the specifically Latvian "witchisation" of the lauma figure under 16th–18th century Christian persecution as a transformation away from the pre-Christian archetype; the Lithuanian tradition preserved the older, more ambivalent supernatural-type more fully.
