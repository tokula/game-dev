# Lauma

> See also: [lauma_baltic_roster.md](lauma_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Laumė (Lithuanian), Lauma (Latvian) — etymology uncertain; possibly from a pre-Baltic substrate word; the name appears in the oldest stratum of Lithuanian folklore, suggesting Mesolithic or Neolithic origin; sometimes glossed as relating to fate-weaving

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian and Latvian oral tradition — pre-Christian; laumių dainos (laumė songs) performed at weddings; compiled in Jonas Basanavičius folk-tale collections (1898–1910) and Krišjānis Barons, *Latvju dainas* (1894–1915)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Gender(s):** Female — universally depicted as female in primary sources; no male Laumės are attested

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants

## One-line lore summary

Female woodland and fate spirits of Baltic mythology, ancient beyond reckoning, who weave destinies, protect orphans and the poor, and consume the men they desire.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Control weather through song, dance, or cursing — can summon hail, storms, and rain at will
- `[major]` Supernatural spinning and weaving at inhuman speed; their textile craft is ritually forbidden to mortal women on specific evenings — Thursday spinning and post-sunset laundering are Laumė prerogatives
- `[minor]` Shapeshifting — can take the form of a mare, goat, bear, or dog; return to humanoid form at will

## Strengths

- Long-lived; treated in folklore as ancient beyond reckoning, possibly predating humanity
- Physically powerful despite feminine appearance
- Deeply skilled in textile work (spinning, weaving, embroidery) — among the finest practitioners in Baltic mythology
- Protective of orphans, abandoned children, and impoverished girls — actively intervene on their behalf

## Characteristics

- Appear as beautiful naked maidens with long fair hair in their primary form
- Older traditions give them bird's claws for feet and the lower body of a goat; some have only one eye (cyclops-like); most traditions describe large breasts with stone nipples
- Inhabit forest stones, abandoned bathhouses, lake shores, river banks, and swamps — always near water
- Gather at night near water during new moon and full moon
- Cannot bear children; yearn deeply for them — this unfulfilled longing drives them to steal human infants
- Their spinning and weaving follows a sacred ritual schedule tied to days of the week

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Cannot give birth; this biological lack is the root of their most dangerous behavior (child-stealing)
- Fear iron tools — iron repels or injures them
- Extremely vindictive when shown disrespect, particularly by men

## Modifiers

- Servants of Laima (Baltic goddess of fate and luck) — *divine authority relationship; Laumės carry out Laima's decrees regarding human destiny*
- In some traditions a Laumė was wife of Perkūnas (thunder god) — *mythological origin; contested across sources — see Sources note*

## Ecology

**Prey:** Human men (lured through desire, loved until exhausted, then consumed); human infants (stolen to raise as surrogate children — not consumed)

**Predators:** Iron tools (primary ward against them); Perkūnas in traditions where they are enemies; armed or ritually prepared mortals

---

> **Note on sources:** Laumės represent one of the oldest strata of Baltic mythology; some scholars place their origin in the Mesolithic period. Primary documentation is 19th-century folklore compilation, meaning the oral tradition was transmitted for centuries through Christianization before being recorded. The monstrous physical traits (bird claws, stone nipples, single eye) appear in older tale variants and were softened in later romanticized retellings. The relationship to Perkūnas is inconsistent across sources: some *dainos* call a Laumė his wife; folk tales often cast Laumės as enemies he must subdue.
