# Sidhe (Aos Sí / Fair Folk)

> See also: [sidhe_celtic_roster.md](sidhe_celtic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** *Aos Sí* (Old Irish: *aos* = "folk, people", *sí/síde* = "otherworldly mound, hollow hill"; Modern Irish: *Aos Sí*; Scottish Gaelic: *Daoine Sìthe*, "people of the mounds"; the anglicised "Sidhe" /ʃiː/ derives from the genitive form of *síde* used as a common noun; "fair folk" is an English euphemism — "fair" meaning both beautiful and well-disposed — used to avoid naming them directly and thereby risk offence)

**Source Layer:** Celtic

**Primary origin:** Irish mythology and oral folk tradition — *Lebor Gabála Érenn* (Book of Invasions, compiled c. 11th century) for the establishment of the mounds; *De Gabáil in t-Sída* (Taking of the Fairy Mound, c. Medieval) for the allocation of specific mounds; *Tochmarc Étaíne* (c. 8th–12th century) for otherworld time and a sídhe lord's cross-world pursuit; *Acallam na Senórach* (c. 12th century) for retrospective Fenian encounters with sídhe folk

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Male and Female — male sídhe lords (Midir, Aengus, Bodb Derg) and female beings (the bean sídhe, sovereignty figures) are both attested; no gender restriction on domain or power within the Sidhe

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Corrupted/exiled

## One-line lore summary

The divine race of Ireland withdrawn into the hollow hills — immortal, beautiful, and capricious neighbours who bless those who respect their mounds and blight those who do not.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Time dilation in the sídhe — mortal time runs independently from otherworld time; those who enter the sídhe may emerge to find years or centuries have passed outside; this is an intrinsic property of the realm, not a skill, and cannot be replicated by mortal means (*Tochmarc Étaíne* — Étaín exists in the otherworld without aging across mortal generations)
- `[major]` Féth fíada (veil of concealment) — the Sidhe can render themselves, their mounds, and any individual or group under their protection invisible to mortal sight; sustained invisibility of this scale is unavailable to mortal races; the Milesians were granted half of Ireland underground partly because the TDD retained this veil
- `[major]` Abduction and glamour — the Sidhe can draw mortals into the sídhe through irresistible music, compelling beauty, or direct compulsion; once inside, the mortal loses time and cannot leave without the goodwill of the Sidhe lord of that mound
- `[minor]` Agricultural luck influence — can bless or blight crops, livestock, and individual human fortune through proximity and disposition; not dramatic weather-working, but sustained incremental effect on rural communities that depend on their good favour

## Strengths

- Immortality within the sídhe — the Sidhe do not age inside their realm; individual lifespans in the mortal world are extreme but primary sources do not characterize them as literally undying under all conditions
- Musical mastery — Sidhe music is irresistibly beautiful; mortals who hear it are drawn to dance and cannot stop voluntarily until exhausted or released; it operates across the boundary between mortal and otherworldly space
- Equestrian excellence — the Sidhe ride horses of supernatural speed and endurance; fairy mounts can cross water, traverse between mounds, and carry a mortal to the otherworld within a single night

## Characteristics

- Mound-dwelling — the Sidhe inhabit specific named *sídhe* (hollow hills, Neolithic mounds) allocated by the Dagda and governed by Manannán mac Lir after the Milesian settlement; each lord holds a distinct, non-overlapping territory (*De Gabáil in t-Sída*)
- Beautiful appearance — consistently described as luminous and compelling; "fair folk" denotes their beauty; individual lords range from radiant (Aengus as god of youth and love) to severe (battle-adjacent figures)
- Reactive guardianship — the Sidhe primarily disturb mortals who transgress against their mounds, rings, paths, and sacred trees (especially hawthorn); they are not aggressive without provocation
- Feast culture — feasting, music, and revelry are the primary recorded activities inside the sídhe; accepting food or a permanent gift from the Sidhe binds a mortal to the realm
- Continuous with the Tuatha Dé Danann — the Sidhe are the TDD in their post-defeat form; their individual lords retain the identities and powers they held in the mythological era; the sídhe existence is withdrawal, not transformation

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Bound underground — the terms of the Milesian settlement confine the Sidhe to the sídhe and the otherworld; they cannot rule the surface of Ireland as they once did; their sovereignty magic now operates indirectly through mortal rulers rather than through direct kingship (*Lebor Gabála Érenn*)
- Provocation-dependency — their power over mortal lives is reactive; a mortal community that observes the avoidances faces no inherent Sidhe threat; the Sidhe must be offended or petitioned before they act
- Receding influence — the *Acallam na Senórach* frames the Sidhe as belonging to an earlier age whose direct power has diminished; the retrospective framing suggests their relationship with the mortal world is attenuating over time

## Modifiers

- Exile from the surface — defeated by the Milesians and bound by the terms of their surrender to dwell underground; cannot reclaim surface sovereignty without violating the cosmic compact that ended the mythological age — *origin: terms of the Milesian victory, as recorded in Lebor Gabála Érenn*

## Encounter conditions

| Mode | Trigger / Condition | Protocol | Source |
|------|---------------------|----------|--------|
| Sídhe entry | Mortal crosses the threshold of an open mound, particularly at liminal times | Time inside runs independently of mortal time; the mortal must not eat Sidhe food or accept a permanent gift; departure requires the goodwill of the lord of that mound | *Tochmarc Étaíne* (time dislocation across mortal eras); *De Gabáil in t-Sída* (individual lords hold specific mounds with distinct domains) |
| Fidchell compact | A Sidhe lord initiates a formal board-game challenge with a mortal ruler | Victory creates an enforceable supernatural debt binding on the mortal under otherworldly law; the terms are declared before play; refusal to honor the debt authorizes the Sidhe lord to collect by force or transformation | *Tochmarc Étaíne* (Midir and Eochaid Airem — attested for this lord specifically; generalization to Sidhe custom is plausible but not universally stated) |

> **Note:** A third commonly attested encounter mode — mound disturbance triggering Sidhe retaliation (illness, misfortune, or harassment) — is widely documented in Irish and Scottish Gaelic folk tradition but is not explicitly codified as a trigger-protocol-consequence rule in any single medieval primary text accessible here. The territorial ownership of mounds is primary-source attested (*De Gabáil in t-Sída*); the specific retaliation mechanism is folk tradition. Both are noted here for completeness but the latter is not given a formal Encounter conditions row.

## Ecology

**Prey:** N/A — the Sidhe sustain themselves on otherworldly feasting (*Fled Goibhnenn*, the Dagda's cauldron) rather than mundane prey; no mortal-world hunting is attested

**Predators:** N/A in the sídhe realm; in the mortal world, Sidhe lords can be challenged by heroes of sufficient divine lineage (attested across Ulster Cycle and Fenian cycle material), but this is individual narrative conflict rather than ecological predation

> **Note on sources:** The Sidhe and the Tuatha Dé Danann share identity in the primary sources — the TDD became the Sidhe when they withdrew into the mounds after the Milesian settlement (*Lebor Gabála Érenn*). This file focuses on the Sidhe as a distinct game-design archetype: the mound-dwelling, mortal-adjacent, folk-tradition face of the same divine race. Their named individuals, detailed individual distinctions, and mythological-era war roles are documented in [tuatha_de_danann_celtic.md](tuatha_de_danann_celtic.md) and its roster.
