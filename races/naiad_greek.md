# Naiads

> See also: [naiad_greek_roster.md](naiad_greek_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Ναϊάδες (*Naïádes*); singular Ναϊάς (*Naïás*); from ναίειν (*naíein*, "to flow"); referring to their nature as spirits of flowing water.

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology — attested throughout Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* (c. 8th century BCE) and Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE); individual Naiad myths extensively documented in Ovid's *Metamorphoses* (c. 8 CE).

**Biological Form:** Humanoid (semi-divine)

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Female

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Nature spirits

## One-line lore summary

The divine maidens of freshwater — guardians of springs, rivers, streams, and lakes — who heal the sick who bathe in their waters, inspire mortal seekers, and are relentlessly pursued by gods and satyrs alike.

## Specializations

- `[minor]` **Healing waters** — the springs and pools Naiads inhabit carry restorative or purifying properties; sanctuaries built around sacred springs functioned as healing sites throughout the Greek world, the spring's potency understood as the Naiad's gift.
- `[minor]` **Nympholepsy** — a mortal who encounters a Naiad in her sacred spring and comes under her influence may enter an ecstatic, prophetic, or inspired state (*nympholēpsia*); the mortal becomes "nymph-seized," gaining temporary prophetic gifts at the cost of ordinary rationality; attested in Plato's *Phaedrus* (238c–d), where Socrates himself invokes the condition.

## Strengths

- Long-lived relative to mortals; some traditions classify them as immortal, others as dying only if their water source fails.
- Divine beauty compelling to mortals, gods, and giants.
- Local knowledge: a Naiad's awareness extends through her entire water source — she perceives those who enter her waters or approach her spring.

## Characteristics

- Bound to a specific body of freshwater (a spring, stream, river, or lake); their identity and wellbeing are tied to that water source.
- Appear as beautiful young women; no distinctive physical features separate them from mortals — the divine nature is felt, not seen.
- Prolific participants in Greek mythological genealogy: Naiads are mothers of heroes, founders, and regional rulers across many traditions; the daughters of river-god Naiads are themselves often Naiads.
- Mortal fathers are common: many Naiads are daughters of river gods (Potamoi) who are themselves sons of the Titans Oceanus and Tethys.

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Tied to their water source: if the spring dries up or the river is diverted, the Naiad may weaken or die; their existence is contingent on the health of the water.
- Vulnerable to pursuit: Naiads are frequently sought by Olympians, satyrs, and mortal men; their relative power compared to gods makes evasion the main recourse.

## Modifiers

None attested as a class.
