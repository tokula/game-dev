# Velnias

> See also: [velnias_baltic_roster.md](velnias_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Velnias (Lithuanian), Vels / Velns (Latvian) — from Proto-Baltic *\*Welnas*, sharing a root with *vėlė* ("shade of the dead") and possibly also Proto-IE *\*wel-* ("to see, to divine"); the same root yields the Slavic Veles and Vedic Varuṇa cognates in some reconstructions

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian and Latvian oral tradition — *dainos* (folk songs), chthonic rites, and folk tales; earliest written reference in Jan Łasicki, *De diis Samagitarum caeterorumque Sarmatarum et falsorum Christianorum* (1582); further documented in Konstanty Szyrwid, *Dictionarium Trium Linguarum* (1629)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Gender(s):** Male in primary form; no stable physical form — shapeshifts freely across sexes, species, and professional archetypes

## One-line lore summary

The one-eyed trickster lord of the Baltic underworld — patron of cattle, magic, and the dead — who perpetually steals from the celestial gods and is perpetually hunted by Perkūnas's lightning.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Absolute sovereign of the realm of the dead — receives every Vėlė (soul of the dead) and governs the entire population of the Lithuanian underworld without limitation
- `[major]` Shapeshifting with no apparent constraint — can become any animal (serpent, bull, bird, bear), any human archetype (old man, shepherd, blacksmith), or natural phenomenon; this applies to others' perceptions as well as his own form
- `[major]` Prophetic — holds oracular knowledge of past and future; no training or luck in another race could replicate this level of prescience
- `[minor]` Raises whirlwinds; can lead the host of the dead through the night sky as a visible procession

## Strengths

- God of cattle and livestock — his domain over earthly animals makes him patron of husbandry, agriculture, and the material wealth of herds
- Protects the poor and actively punishes evildoers (drunkards, thieves, dishonest individuals)
- Associated with magic, music, and poetry — serves as a patron of bardic and magical arts in some traditions
- Can perform extraordinary feats of construction (bridges, buildings) as a trickster bargain

## Characteristics

- One-eyed — a prophetic attribute; removing this would change what he fundamentally is
- Trickster archetype — deceives, bargains, steals celestial cattle, and confounds other gods
- Dwells in chthonic locations: barns, ponds, caves, marshlands, underground spaces — never in open sky
- His cosmic role is defined by the eternal conflict with Perkūnas: Velnias steals the cattle of the sky gods; Perkūnas hunts him with lightning; Velnias hides in trees, water, animals, and boulders; storms are the visible aftermath of this chase
- Post-Christian tradition conflated him with the Devil, overlaying trickster-evil attributes that may distort his pre-Christian character

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Repelled and harmed by specific plants: rowan, ivy, linden, hazel, juniper; also by candlelight

## Modifiers

- Post-Christian conflation with Piktis (the Baltic devil) and with the Christian Devil — *imposed cultural modification; origin: Christianization of Lithuania from 1387; documented equation in Szyrwid (1629); causes many post-1387 sources to be unreliable guides to pre-Christian Velnias*
- Repulsion by Christian sacred objects (cross, rosary, holy bread) — *imposed overlay; origin: Christian-era folk belief; absent from pre-Christian material*

## Ecology

**Prey:** Cattle of the celestial gods — the mythological cattle-theft motif is his primary attested act of predation; also receives tribute of food and offerings from the living

**Predators:** Perkūnas (perpetually hunts Velnias with lightning bolts across the sky)

> **Note on sources:** Post-1387 written sources (after Lithuania's Christianization) frequently equate Velnias with the Devil and cannot be treated as reliable guides to pre-Christian belief. Szyrwid's 1629 dictionary explicitly conflates Velnias with *Piktis* (a Baltic devil-name). The 19th-century folk material in *dainos* and folk tales is more useful but still Christianized. The cosmic cattle-theft and Perkūnas-Velnias conflict motif is the best-attested pre-Christian element, strongly supported by comparative Baltic-Slavic mythology.
