# Dökkálfar

> See also: [dokkalfar_norse_roster.md](dokkalfar_norse_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Dökkálfar (Old Norse: plural; singular Dökkálfr); from *dökkr* "dark, dim, dusky" + *álfr* "elf." Literally "dark elves." Contrasted explicitly with *Ljósálfar* (light elves, from *ljós* "light"). The broader term *álfar* (elves) appears throughout the Poetic Edda without this light/dark division.

**Source Layer:** Norse

**Primary origin:** Norse mythology — Prose Edda, *Gylfaginning* (Snorri Sturluson, c. 1220). The Ljós/Dökkálfar distinction does not appear in the Poetic Edda.

**Biological Form:** Humanoid

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Unspecified in primary sources — Snorri's description assigns no gender characteristics to either type of elf.

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Corrupted/exiled

## One-line lore summary

The dark-complected, subterranean counterpart to the light elves of Norse cosmology — attested almost entirely through a single brief passage in Snorri's Prose Edda, with no named individuals and a scholarly debate about whether they are a genuine distinct race or a Christianising conflation of older traditions.

## Specializations

None clearly attested in primary sources. Snorri's description does not assign specific magical abilities to the Dökkálfar; the contrast with the Ljósálfar is cosmological and moral rather than functional.

## Strengths

- Subterranean habitation implies familiarity with underground environments and resources, by structural parallel with the Dvergar if the two are related

## Characteristics

- Described by Snorri as "darker than pitch" (*svartari en bik*) in complexion — the defining physical description in the sole primary source
- Inhabit underground realms, explicitly distinct from the Ljósálfar's Álfheimr; if identical to the Svartálfar, their realm is Svartálfaheimr, which overlaps with Niðavellir (the Dvergar's realm) in Snorri's cosmology
- Presented as a cosmological counterweight to the Ljósálfar — the two types are explicitly contrasted but given no narrative interaction or detailed mythology in surviving primary sources

## Faults / Weaknesses

No vulnerabilities specifically attested for the Dökkálfar in primary sources. The sunlight petrification attested for the Dvergar (Álvíssmál) may apply if the Dökkálfar and Dvergar are the same, but this is not confirmed in surviving texts.

## Modifiers

None attested in primary sources.

## Game depictions (variants from the source mythology)

### D&D 5e Dark Elves (Drow) `[SRD]`

The Drow of the D&D 5e Systems Reference Document (CC-BY-4.0) share the underground-dwelling and dark-complected archetype with the Dökkálfar but are a substantially developed independent creation: they possess a formalised matriarchal culture, civilisation-scale underground cities, darkvision, and sunlight sensitivity — none of which are attested in Norse primary sources for the Dökkálfar. The SRD Drow drew loose conceptual inspiration from the Dökkálfar archetype.

---

> **Note on sources:** Snorri's *Gylfaginning* is the sole primary source distinguishing Dökkálfar from Ljósálfar, and the same text also separately names Svartálfar ("black elves"). Modern scholars (including Rudolf Simek, John Lindow, and Gabriel Turville-Petre) debate whether Dökkálfar and Svartálfar are two names for the same race, or genuinely distinct categories in Snorri's scheme. A further complication: Snorri's Svartálfaheimr appears to overlap with Niðavellir (the Dvergar's realm), leading several scholars to conclude that Snorri's Svartálfar — and by extension possibly his Dökkálfar — are simply the Dvergar under an alternative name. The Poetic Edda does not draw the Ljós/Dökkálfar distinction at all, using only undifferentiated *álfar*. | **Snorri's context:** Snorri Sturluson (1179–1241) was an Icelandic chieftain and lawspeaker at the Althing, assassinated on orders of King Hákon IV of Norway — testament to his political significance. He wrote the Prose Edda c. 1220 not as theology but as a *skaldic manual* for court poets who needed mythological literacy to understand kennings. Critically, he was a Christian composing this work approximately 220 years after Iceland's conversion (c. 1000 CE), systematizing an oral tradition that had been filtered through two centuries of Christian culture. The Ljósálfar/Dökkálfar binary — "brighter than the sun" vs. "blacker than pitch" (*svartari en bik*) — maps onto Christian angel/demon dualism in a way the Poetic Edda does not reflect; Simek flags this as likely Christian influence on Snorri's taxonomy. That same phrase *svartari en bik* is used identically for the Svartálfar, with no distinguishing detail between the two. | **Why this profile is maintained separately from Dvergar:** The Dökkálfar are introduced in *Gylfaginning*'s *elf taxonomy* passage — not the section on dwarves or craftsmen — where Snorri's explicit framing positions them as a type of *álfr* contrasting with the Ljósálfar. Even if the underlying beings are the same as the Dvergar, the Dökkálfar entry documents a distinct cosmological function — the light/dark elf axis — that the Dvergar's craftsmen role does not carry. The Svartálfar content (underground craftsmen with no independent cosmological axis) has been merged into [dvergar_norse.md](dvergar_norse.md). This profile is retained because the Ljósálfar contrast is its defining and unique characteristic.
