# Dökkálfar

> See also: [dokkalfar_norse_roster.md](dokkalfar_norse_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Dökkálfar (Old Norse: *dökkr* "dark, gloomy" + *álfar* "elves"; plural of *dökkálfr*; meaning "Dark Elves." The root *dökkr* is cognate with Old English *deorc* and Proto-Germanic *derkaz* "dark.")

**Source Layer:** Norse

**Primary origin:** Norse mythology — Prose Edda, Snorri Sturluson (c. 1220), *Gylfaginning* ch. 17 (only direct primary source)

**Biological Form:** Humanoid

**Gender(s):** Not specified in primary sources.

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Corrupted/exiled

## One-line lore summary

Subterranean, pitch-black beings dwelling underground, described in a single primary-source passage as wholly unlike the radiant Ljósálfar in both appearance and nature.

- Described as "blacker than pitch" (*svartari en kolr*) in appearance

- Distinguished from Ljósálfar in both appearance and *eðli* (nature, character) — the content of this distinction beyond the physical is not elaborated

---

> **Note on sources:** The Dökkálfar are attested in only one passage of one primary source — Snorri Sturluson's *Gylfaginning* (c. 1220). Their relationship to the Svartálfar ("Black Elves," mentioned separately in *Skáldskaparmál*) is actively debated: some scholars treat them as synonymous, others as distinct types, and others argue both categories are Snorri's interpretive gloss on what the older tradition called Dvergar (dwarves). The Poetic Edda (which predates Snorri) makes no light/dark distinction among the *álfar*. Any game or fictional elaboration of Dökkálfar draws heavily on authorial invention beyond what the primary record supports. See also [ljosalfar_norse.md](ljosalfar_norse.md) and [svartalfar_norse.md](svartalfar_norse.md).
