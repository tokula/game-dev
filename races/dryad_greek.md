# Dryads / Hamadryads

> See also: [dryad_greek_roster.md](dryad_greek_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Δρυάδες (*Dryádes*); singular Δρυάς (*Dryás*); from δρῦς (*drys*, "oak," then by extension "tree"); Hamadryad from ἅμα (*hama*, "together with") + δρῦς (*drys*, "tree") — "together-with-tree," describing the bond between nymph and tree. The distinction: Dryads roam freely through forests, while Hamadryads are inseparably bound to a specific tree and perish with it.

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology — Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE) names the Meliae (ash-tree nymphs) as the eldest tree nymphs, born from Uranus's blood (ll. 187–188); the *Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite* (c. 6th–4th century BCE) describes mountain nymphs whose lives are tied to trees and who die when their trees die; individual Dryad myths documented in Ovid's *Metamorphoses* (c. 8 CE).

**Biological Form:** Humanoid / Spirit (plant-bound)

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Female

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Nature spirits

## One-line lore summary

The divine maidens of trees and forest — Dryads who wander freely among the groves, and Hamadryads whose life-force is literally grafted into a single tree and ends the moment it falls.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Tree-bond (Hamadryad)** — a Hamadryad is born with a specific tree and dies with it; the nymph and tree share one life; cutting or burning the tree kills the nymph; attested in the *Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite* (ll. 256–272), which describes how "when the Moira has ordained that they shall die, the lovely trees wither first upon the ground, and the bark decays around them, and the boughs fall off, and at last the soul of the Nymph and the spirit of the tree leave the light of the sun together."
- `[minor]` **Extended lifespan** — tree nymphs are "long-lived" compared to mortals but explicitly not immortal; the *Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite* states they live for "nine generations of long-lived men" alongside their trees (ll. 264–272), which ancient commentators calculated at approximately 9,720 years.

## Strengths

- Deeply attuned to their forest: a Dryad perceives intrusions into her grove; sound, movement, and intention within the trees are known to her.
- The Meliae (ash-tree nymphs), the eldest class, predate the Olympian era — their power in ancient groves may exceed that of younger nature spirits.
- Dryads (as opposed to Hamadryads) can move freely through their forest and coordinate to protect it.

## Characteristics

- **Dryad vs. Hamadryad distinction:** Dryads are associated with trees generally and can move through forests; Hamadryads are bound to a single tree (particularly oak trees in the oldest tradition) and are fully immobile spiritually if not physically. The boundary between the two types is inconsistent across ancient sources.
- The **Meliae** are the eldest tree nymphs — ash-tree spirits born from the drops of Uranus's blood when Cronus castrated him; they precede the Titans and Olympians and belong to the Protogenoi generation's aftermath (*Theogony* ll. 187–188).
- Appear as beautiful young women; their hair may have a leafy quality in later artistic tradition, but primary sources do not describe unusual physical features.
- Mortal-like in their relationship with death: unlike Olympians, Dryads and Hamadryads can die — through their tree's destruction or the end of their natural lifespan.

## Faults / Weaknesses

- **Hamadryad vulnerability:** destruction of the host tree kills the Hamadryad; they are acutely vulnerable to fire, axes, and lightning; their life cannot be separated from the tree's life.
- **Forest dependency:** Dryads are weakest outside forested areas; in cleared land, deserts, or urban settings, they have no domain and lose their connection to the land.

## Modifiers

None attested as a class.

> **Note on sources:** The tree-nymph tradition spans a wide range of ancient authors, and the distinction between Dryads (free-ranging) and Hamadryads (tree-bound) is not always sharp in primary texts. The *Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite* (ll. 256–272) gives the clearest primary-source account of tree-nymph lifespan and tree-bond: it describes nymphs who "dwell with" trees that "stand and flourish as a man's life" — clearly distinguishing them from the immortal Olympians. The Meliae of Hesiod's *Theogony* are sometimes treated separately from Dryads, as they predate the Olympian generation and are connected to the iron race of men (born from the ash tree) in *Works and Days*.
