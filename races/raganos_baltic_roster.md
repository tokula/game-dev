# Raganos — Baltic Roster

> See also: [raganos_baltic.md](raganos_baltic.md)

## Command hierarchy

Raganos gather collectively under the authority of the goddess Ragana ([ragana_baltic.md](ragana_baltic.md)) at Winter Solstice. No internal hierarchy among the raganos themselves is documented in primary sources — they appear to function as a coordinated collective under the goddess rather than a ranked organization. Individual raganos operate autonomously within their local territory between gatherings. The male variant (*raganius / ragainis*) exists in folk sources but holds no documented authority over female raganos.

**Source priority:** Primary sources (folklore, mythology, religious texts, historical accounts) are the authoritative origin. Games and fiction derive from them. Always populate `— Primary sources` first and most completely. Derivative sections exist for cross-reference only — they should never displace or reframe the original cultural record.

**Section naming rule:** Use `— Primary sources` when the race has a real-world folkloric or mythological base.

## Named individuals — Primary sources

Folk sources consistently describe raganos as a type rather than naming individuals. The oral tradition preserves behaviors, abilities, and encounters, not personal names of specific raganos. No individual ragana is named and attested across multiple primary sources.

| Name | Source | Rank / role | Notes |
|------|--------|-------------|-------|
| Raganos (collective) | Lithuanian/Latvian *dainos* and *pasakos*, oral tradition | Witch-beings; cosmic balance-enforcers; Winter Solstice gatherers | No individual names recorded in the primary oral tradition; the type is attested but individuals are anonymous |

## Named individuals — Modern literary

| Name | Work | Role | Notes |
|------|------|------|-------|
| Spīdola | *Lāčplēsis*, Andrejs Pumpurs, 1888 (Latvian folk epic — PD, Pumpurs died 1902) | Ragana-type sorceress; initially antagonist, reverses to ally against German invaders | The most prominent individually-named ragana figure in PD Latvian literary tradition; demonstrates the balance-reversal motif — her dark impulses yield to a higher alignment; often cited as a model for morally complex ragana characterization |
