# Titans

> See also: [titan_greek_roster.md](titan_greek_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Τιτᾶνες (*Titānes*); Hesiod derives the name in the *Theogony* (ll. 207–210) from τιταίνω (*titaínō*, "to strain, overreach") and τίσις (*tísis*, "retribution") as a folk etymology for their punishment; the underlying pre-Greek etymology remains uncertain.

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology — Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE) is the canonical account; supplemented by *Works and Days* (c. 700 BCE), Aeschylus's *Prometheus Bound* (c. 430 BCE), and Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* (c. 1st–2nd century CE).

**Biological Form:** Humanoid (divine)

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Male and Female

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine rulers, Corrupted/exiled

## One-line lore summary

The elder divine generation of Greek cosmology — cosmic sovereigns who ruled the primordial world before being overthrown and imprisoned in Tartarus after a ten-year war against the Olympians they themselves had raised.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Immortality** — Titans cannot age or die by natural means; they may be imprisoned or bound but not destroyed.
- `[major]` **Cosmic domain sovereignty** — each Titan embodies and rules a fundamental cosmic principle (the world-encircling ocean, divine memory, sacred law, heavenly light, etc.) whose operation persists through their person even in imprisonment.

## Strengths

- Immense physical might; Atlas sustains the sky on his shoulders — a burden explicitly beyond Olympian capacity.
- Primordial authority: divine laws and oaths established under Titan rule (particularly those governed by Themis) remained binding through and after the Olympian succession.

## Characteristics

- First true divine generation with defined individual roles: children of Uranus (Sky) and Gaia (Earth); the twelve original Titans number six male and six female (*Theogony* ll. 133–138).
- Presided over the Golden Age of humanity (*Works and Days* ll. 111–120), when mortals lived free from toil, illness, and suffering.
- Described in primary sources as immense in scale relative to Olympians, though specific measurements are not given.

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Defeated in the Titanomachy — the ten-year war against the Olympians — and imprisoned in Tartarus behind bronze gates and walls, guarded by the Hecatoncheires (*Theogony* ll. 717–735).
- Subject to divine succession: the sovereignty of each generation can be superseded by the next; the Titans themselves overthrew Uranus before being overthrown in turn.

## Modifiers

- **Imprisonment in Tartarus** — decreed by Zeus following his victory in the Titanomachy; applies to those Titans who fought against him. Titans who sided with Zeus (Prometheus, Oceanus, and the Titanesses Themis, Mnemosyne, Rhea, and Tethys) were exempt. — *Theogony*, Hesiod, ll. 717–735

> **Note on sources:** Hesiod's *Theogony* (ll. 133–138) lists twelve Titans as children of Uranus and Gaia. Apollodorus (*Bibliotheca* 1.1.3) follows Hesiod but some manuscript variants add Dione as a thirteenth. Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* refer to the Titans collectively without enumerating individuals. Second-generation children of Titans — Helios, Selene, Eos, Prometheus, Atlas, and others — are sometimes classed as Titans in later sources, though Hesiod does not apply the term to them consistently.
