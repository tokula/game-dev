# Daimones

> See also: [daimon_greek_roster.md](daimon_greek_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Δαίμονες (*Daimones*); singular δαίμων (*daímōn*); etymology uncertain — most likely from Proto-Indo-European *deh₂-* meaning "to divide, to distribute," reflecting their role as dispensers of fate and fortune (*Works and Days*, Hesiod).

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology and philosophy — Hesiod's *Works and Days* (c. 700 BCE) establishes the concept of daimones as Golden Age souls transformed into guardians; Plato's *Symposium* (c. 385–370 BCE) defines them as intermediaries between the divine and mortal realms.

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Male and Female (though most are conceived as incorporeal and effectively genderless; named daimones of abstract concepts may be male or female according to grammatical gender)

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants, Nature spirits

## One-line lore summary

The vast category of spirit beings intermediate between Olympians and mortals — dispensers of fate and fortune who move unseen among humans as guardians, messengers, and enforcers of divine will.

## Specializations

- `[major]` **Incorporeal movement** — daimones move unseen through the mortal world; Hesiod specifies they are "enveloped in mist" and invisible to mortals as they carry out their guardian functions (*Works and Days* ll. 122–126).
- `[minor]` **Fate-dispensing** — daimones assign fortune and destiny to individual mortals; the personal daimon (assigned at birth by lot) oversees a mortal's life from birth to death; attested in Plato's *Republic* (620d–e) and *Phaedo* (107d).

## Strengths

- Serve as living bridges between Olympians and mortals: they carry prayers upward and divine gifts downward (*Symposium*, Plato, 202e).
- Pervasive presence — 30,000 daimones wander the earth according to Hesiod, ensuring comprehensive divine oversight of mortal affairs.
- Adaptable in function: a single daimon may serve as guardian, bringer of prosperity, enforcer of justice, or messenger depending on context.

## Characteristics

- **Intermediate nature:** occupy a position between immortal gods and mortal humans — neither fully divine nor mortal, but sharing qualities of both (*Symposium*, Plato, 202d–203a).
- **Invisibility by default:** move unseen among mortals; Hesiod describes them as "guardians of mortal men, watching over them unseen" (*Works and Days* ll. 121–126); only exceptional mortals or those in certain ritual states perceive them.
- **Moral spectrum:** Hellenistic thought divided daimones into agathodaímōn (εὐδαίμων — "good daimon," bringing prosperity) and kakodaímōn (κακοδαίμων — "malevolent daimon," bringing misfortune); the distinction is attested in common Greek words for "happiness" (eudaimonia) and "misfortune."
- **Heterogeneous origins:** daimones arise through multiple pathways — transformed souls of the golden generation (Hesiod), direct divine appointment (Plato), or emergence as cosmic personifications; the category is a functional class, not a single metaphysical type.
- **Personal assignment:** Plato's tradition holds that each mortal receives a personal daimon before birth, chosen by lot, who accompanies them through life and after death guides the soul through judgment (*Republic* 620d–e).

## Faults / Weaknesses

No universal faults across the category. Individual daimones carry specific limitations appropriate to their function — a daimon of misfortune may be unable to bestow good luck; a daimon bound to a specific mortal dies with that mortal's soul in some traditions.

## Modifiers

- **Origin as Golden Age souls** — Hesiod's *Works and Days* (ll. 109–126) establishes that the golden race of mortals, after death, became daimones appointed as guardians of mortal men by Zeus; this applies only to the Golden-Age-soul subtype, not to daimones of other origins. — *Works and Days*, Hesiod

---

> **Note on sources:** The term *daimon* covers a wide semantic range in ancient sources and does not describe a single unified category of being. Homer uses *daimon* and *theos* (god) nearly interchangeably in the *Iliad*, applying it to Olympians acting incognito or to divine forces of unspecified identity. Hesiod narrows the term to transformed Golden Age souls. Plato broadens it philosophically to all intermediate beings. The Hellenistic elaboration of good/evil daimones reflects later philosophical systematisation rather than a single early tradition. This profile captures the shared characteristics across these uses; readers should treat the category as an ontological class rather than a single species.
