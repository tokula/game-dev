# Giltinė

> See also: [giltine_baltic_roster.md](giltine_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Giltinė (Lithuanian) — from *gelti* ("to sting, to hurt"); literally "the hurter" or "the stinger"; regional variants: Kaulinyčia ("bone woman"), Maras, Maro mergė ("plague maiden")

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition — *dainos* (folk songs) and folk narratives; documented in Jonas Basanavičius, *Iš gyvenimo vėlių bei velnių* (1903–1904); mentioned in Mikalojus Daukša, *Catechismus* (1595); preserved in A. Juška, *Lietuviškos dainos* (folk song collections)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Gender(s):** Female

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants (death executor, psychopomp)

## One-line lore summary

The tall, skeletal Lithuanian goddess of death — sister of Laima — who wanders cemeteries at night and delivers each person's fated end with a poisonous tongue.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Poisonous tongue — her lick or touch kills without fail; this is not venom but a death-force intrinsic to her being; no antidote exists in Lithuanian folk tradition
- `[major]` Passes through any barrier to reach the dying — walls, locks, and wards do not stop her once the fated hour has arrived
- `[minor]` Shapeshifting — can appear as a beautiful young woman or a white-shrouded figure in addition to her skeletal default form

## Strengths

- Cannot be slain while her cosmic function continues — she is immune to death by the nature of her office
- Knows the precise moment of each mortal's fated death (operating in tandem with Laima, who sets the hour)
- Moves freely in darkness without constraint; cemetery grounds are her uncontested domain

## Characteristics

- Skeletal form with blue-tinged skin, white or grey hair; teeth of iron in some accounts
- Wanders cemeteries at night; licks the coffins or door-posts of the dying to signal and deliver the soul's release
- Sacred bird: owl — her arrival near a household is announced by owl calls
- Works in concert with Laima: Laima determines the fate; Giltinė executes it
- Carries a bottle of death-potion in one hand and a length of thread (representing the life-span) in the other, in some variant accounts

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Bound by Laima's decree — she cannot claim a soul before its fated hour regardless of proximity; this is an absolute constraint
- Correct mortuary ritual and sacred fire create a recognized boundary she respects; she cannot be blocked, but the rites establish a formal compact

## Modifiers

- Subordinate to Laima in the hierarchy of fate — *cosmic divine order; origin: Lithuanian folk belief; Giltinė acts as executor of Laima's decrees and cannot override them*
- Post-Christian conflation with demonic death figures — *imposed cultural overlay; origin: Christianization of Lithuania from 1387; distorts pre-Christian characterization in sources dated after that point*

**Prey:** Mortals at their fated hour — Giltinė harvests souls, not bodies; her lick releases the soul from the dying body

---

> **Note on sources:** Post-1387 written sources are affected by Lithuanian Christianization and tend to conflate Giltinė with demonic death figures or merge her with Maras (plague deity). The most reliable pre-Christian characterization comes from the *dainos* folk song tradition compiled in the 19th century, which preserves the sister-relationship with Laima and the iconic licking/poisonous-tongue motif. The regional name Kaulinyčia ("bone woman") may represent a separate local death spirit or an alternate manifestation of the same archetype — sources do not resolve this clearly.
