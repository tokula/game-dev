# Nereids — Greek Roster

> See also: [nereid_greek.md](nereid_greek.md)

## Command hierarchy

The Nereids are subject to their father Nereus ("the Old Man of the Sea") and to Poseidon as lord of their domain. Amphitrite, as Poseidon's queen, holds a position of authority above the other Nereids within Poseidon's court. Among themselves, the Nereids act as a collective without formal rank — in primary sources they appear as a group in mourning or celebration rather than in any hierarchical arrangement.

**Source priority:** Hesiod's *Theogony* (ll. 243–262) and Homer's *Iliad* (Bk 18.35–51) are the primary sources. Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* and Hyginus's *Fabulae* supplement with individual myths.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

**Amphitrite**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Queenship of the sea — as Poseidon's consort, Amphitrite governs the sea court alongside him; she mediates between mortals appealing to sea-gods and Poseidon himself; her authority over sea creatures (particularly dolphins, who brought her to Poseidon) is attested
- `[Modifier]` Unwilling marriage — Amphitrite initially fled Poseidon's advances and was found only through the dolphin's mediation; the marriage is not freely chosen; attested in various sources

**Galatea**
- `[Characteristic]` Object of unrequited divine obsession — Polyphemus the Cyclops courts her with pastoral songs while she loves the mortal Acis; she is entirely indifferent to Polyphemus's advances; the story is the archetypal unrequited love narrative in Greek literature

**Nemertes**
- `[Strength]` Inherited truth-telling — Hesiod explicitly states she "has the mind of her immortal father" (Nereus, who never lies); she carries the truth-telling faculty as a personal trait, not merely a family trait

**Psamathe**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Shapeshifting — Psamathe transformed into a seal to escape Aeacus's pursuit; Aeacus held on regardless and she resumed her form; the seal transformation is the defining feature of her myth (*Bibliotheca*, Apollodorus, 3.12.6)
- `[Modifier]` Grief over Phocus — after Peleus (Aeacus's son) accidentally or deliberately killed her son Phocus, Psamathe sent a wolf to ravage Peleus's cattle; Thetis interceded; the wolf was turned to stone; this makes Psamathe the closest thing to an antagonist Nereid in attested myth (*Bibliotheca*, Apollodorus)

**Thetis**
- `[Specialization-major]` Prophetic knowledge — Thetis knows her son Achilles is fated to choose between a long obscure life and a short glorious one; she acts on this knowledge to protect him, first by disguising him among girls on Skyros, then by equipping him with divine armour; her foreknowledge is treated as reliable throughout the *Iliad*
- `[Specialization-minor]` Shapeshifting — when Peleus seized her, Thetis transformed through fire, water, wind, and beast forms to escape; Peleus held on through all transformations until she submitted; attested in Pindar's *Odes*
- `[Modifier]` Forced marriage to Peleus — a prophecy warned that Thetis's son would surpass his father in greatness; to prevent this from threatening Zeus's supremacy, she was made to marry a mortal; the marriage is explicitly against her will — *Theogony*, Hesiod; *Odes*, Pindar
- `[Fault]` Cannot save Achilles — despite divine power and foreknowledge, Thetis cannot override the fate that will kill her son; her grief is the emotional anchor of the *Iliad*'s final books
