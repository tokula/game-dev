# Kami

> See also: [kami_east_asian_roster.md](kami_east_asian_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** 神 (kami) — the character means "god, spirit, divine being"; etymology contested; Motoori Norinaga's classical definition: "any being whatsoever which possesses some eminent quality out of the ordinary, and is awe-inspiring"; possible connection to Ainu *kamuy* noted but not settled in scholarship

**Source Layer:** East Asian (Japanese)

**Primary origin:** Japanese Shinto tradition; systematically recorded in *Kojiki* (712 CE) and *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE); ritual practice codified in *Engishiki* (compiled 907–927 CE); worshipped and invoked in *Man'yōshū* (compiled c. 759 CE)

**Biological Form:** Spirit / Elemental

**Entity type:** Race/Species

> **Note:** Kami is a broad divine category — not a single species but a class of spiritual presences that inhabit natural phenomena, sacred places, and living things. The Engishiki (927 CE) lists 2,861 shrine kami; later tradition counts ya-o-yorozu no kami (八百万の神, "eight million kami") as a symbolic expression of their countless number. The roster covers the major named kami attested in Kojiki and Nihon Shoki.

**Gender(s):** Male and Female — both extensively attested; creator deities are paired (Izanagi / Izanami); major kami include female (Amaterasu, Izanami) and male (Susanoo, Ōkuninushi, Takemikazuchi) members; some kami are not gendered or take multiple forms

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Divine servants, Warrior culture, Scholarly

## One-line lore summary

The living presences of Shinto — divine spirits inhabiting natural forces, sacred spaces, and the lineage of the Japanese land — who sustain creation through reciprocal covenant with humanity.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Domain mastery** — each kami possesses sovereign authority over their specific domain (sun, storm, harvest, sea, thunder, etc.); phenomena within that domain are direct expressions of the kami's will and state; this authority cannot be replicated by any other being (*Kojiki*, 712 CE; *Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE)
- `[major]` **Dual soul (nigi-mitama / ara-mitama)** — every kami contains a gentle soul (*nigi-mitama*, bestowing blessing, growth, and calm) and an assertive soul (*ara-mitama*, producing storm, plague, and catastrophe); both aspects are real and may manifest independently; the same kami who protects in one aspect destroys in another (*Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE)
- `[major]` **Form adoption (yorishiro)** — kami manifest within natural objects, animals, sacred regalia (*shinki*), or living persons without displacing the host; the host gains divine power or becomes a vessel for the kami's will for the duration of the manifestation (*Kojiki*, 712 CE; *Engishiki*, 927 CE)
- `[minor]` **Divine lineage transmission** — powerful kami pass domain authority and divine power through descent; the imperial line of Japan is explicitly descended from Amaterasu through Ninigi-no-Mikoto (*Kojiki*, 712 CE)

## Strengths

- Unsurpassed knowledge and power within their own domain
- Not bound by mortal lifespan — kami persist indefinitely unless diminished, killed, or forced into a lesser state
- Strengthened by worship — devotion and offerings directly increase a kami's power and presence; a well-venerated kami becomes more capable over time (*Engishiki*, 927 CE)

## Characteristics

- No fixed physical form — inhabit sacred spaces, natural phenomena, and objects rather than possessing independent bodies; appearance varies with manifestation mode and is often perceived indirectly
- Flawed personalities — kami possess emotions, jealousy, rage, and desire comparable to humans; they are not omnipotent or morally infallible (*Kojiki*, 712 CE; *Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE)
- Reciprocal covenant with humans — humans are obligated to keep kami venerated through ritual and offering; kami are obligated to perform the function of their domain; the relationship is structured mutual dependency, not servitude in either direction
- Hierarchical order — Amatsukami (heavenly kami, from Takamagahara) hold authority over Kunitsukami (earthly kami of the land); within the Amatsukami, Amaterasu holds supreme authority (*Kojiki*, 712 CE)
- Pollution sensitivity (*kegare*) — kami are averse to ritual impurity; contact with death, blood, or persistent uncleanliness can diminish their presence or drive them from a location (*Engishiki*, 927 CE)

## Faults / Weaknesses

- **Ara-mitama instability** — the assertive soul can manifest as destructive wrath if the kami is neglected, dishonoured, or provoked by pollution; this aspect does not distinguish between guilty and innocent; unappeasable until proper ritual is completed (*Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE)
- **Domain limitation** — power and knowledge are concentrated in the kami's own domain; outside it they are significantly diminished
- **Pollution repulsion (*kegare*)** — sustained ritual impurity in a location drives kami away and weakens their ability to manifest; a neglected or defiled shrine loses its kami presence (*Engishiki*, 927 CE)
- **Death is possible** — Izanami dies in childbirth; several kami are killed or permanently diminished in the Kojiki's conflict narratives; kami are powerful but not indestructible (*Kojiki*, 712 CE)

## Modifiers

- **Imperial descent covenant** — the heavenly kami (Amatsukami) are bound by an accord established in Takamagahara to support and protect the imperial line descended from Amaterasu through Ninigi-no-Mikoto; this is presented as a structural obligation of the divine order rather than a choice — *Kojiki* (712 CE)
- **Shrine binding** — a kami ritually enshrined in a specific location is partly bound to that site; they can be petitioned, subdivided into branch shrines (*bunsha*), or — in extreme cases — moved to a new location by ritual transfer — *Engishiki* (927 CE)

## Encounter conditions

| Mode | Trigger / Condition | Protocol | Source |
|------|---------------------|----------|--------|
| Shrine approach (参拝, sanpai) | A mortal approaches a kami's enshrined presence to petition or give thanks | Purify at the temizuya (hand-washing basin); bow twice, clap twice (*kashiwade*), bow once; state one's name and offering; absence of purification constitutes pollution and may provoke the kami's ara-mitama | *Engishiki* (927 CE) |
| Norito petition | A mortal or priest formally petitions a kami for a specific blessing or intervention | Recite the correct norito formula for the specific kami and purpose; use of wrong or incomplete formulas may fail to reach the kami or attract the wrong aspect | *Engishiki* (927 CE) |
| Niiname-sai (新嘗祭, first fruits festival) | The new harvest season; the kami of agriculture must be formally offered the first rice of the year | The emperor presents rice, fish, fruit, soup, and stew to the kami before consuming the harvest himself; failure to perform the rite risks withdrawal of agricultural blessing | *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE); *Engishiki* (927 CE) |
| Misogi purification | Before approaching a kami after exposure to death, blood, or impurity (*kegare*) | Ritual bathing in a river or the sea; without misogi, a polluted person cannot safely approach a kami and risks driving the kami away or provoking their ara-mitama | *Kojiki* (712 CE); *Engishiki* (927 CE) |

## Ecology

**Prey:** N/A — kami sustain themselves through devotion and offerings (rice, fish, fruit, sake, cloth, sacred music); they do not hunt; agricultural kami are fed by the harvest they protect

**Predators:** N/A — no biological predator; kami can be weakened by sustained ritual neglect, severe pollution, or conflict with more powerful kami; in the historically syncretic Nara–Heian period, Buddhist doctrine presented a theological challenge to kami authority, though the two traditions eventually merged in *shinbutsu-shūgō*

> **Note on sources:** The *Kojiki* and *Nihon Shoki* were compiled at imperial command partly to legitimise the ruling dynasty's descent from Amaterasu; their accounts of kami are simultaneously religious record and political document. The Engishiki reflects a later Heian-period institutionalisation of Shinto practice. The *Man'yōshū* preserves popular and poetic kami invocations outside the official chronicle tradition. Cross-referencing these sources reveals both the stable core of kami tradition and the variation introduced by political and regional context.
