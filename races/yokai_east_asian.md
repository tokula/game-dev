# Yōkai

> See also: [yokai_east_asian_roster.md](yokai_east_asian_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** 妖怪 (yōkai) — two kanji both meaning "suspicious, doubtful, strange"; derived from Chinese *yāoguài* (妖怪). Shapeshifting variants known as *bakemono* (化け物, "changed thing") or *obake* (お化け). Also rendered *mononoke* (物の怪, "vengeful spirit-thing") in classical texts, though the terms carry distinct nuances.

**Source Layer:** East Asian (Japanese)

**Primary origin:** Japanese oral tradition and Buddhist-influenced literature; anomalous phenomena of this kind are recorded in *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE) and *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE); the term and its typology are codified in *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) and subsequent Heian–Kamakura setsuwa collections

**Biological Form:** Spirit / Aberration

**Entity type:** Race/Species

> **Note:** Yōkai is an umbrella category rather than a single species — it encompasses hundreds of distinct supernatural types including fox spirits (*kitsune*), demons (*oni*), winged mountain beings (*tengu*), water sprites (*kappa*), animated objects (*tsukumogami*), serpentine monsters, and more. The traits below describe the category as a whole. Individual subtypes can be profiled separately for more specific game-design use.

**Gender(s):** Varied — yōkai of all genders are attested in primary sources; some types are exclusively female (e.g. serpent-women), some predominantly male (e.g. tengu, oni); many are sexless, gender-indeterminate, or shift freely with form

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Corrupted/exiled, Divine servants, Nomadic

## One-line lore summary

A vast umbrella of Japanese supernatural entities — spirits, monsters, transformed beings, and animated objects — occupying the liminal space between the divine and the profane, ranging from benevolent protectors to lethal predators.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Shapeshifting (bakemono / obake)** — many yōkai can assume any human, animal, or object form at will; the transformation is instantaneous and indistinguishable from the genuine article; no other species can replicate this through training (*Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- `[major]` **Spiritual possession (tsuki-mono)** — ability to inhabit and control a human or animal host, inducing sickness, madness, or compelled behaviour; the host is unaware and cannot self-expel the spirit (*Nihon Ryōiki*, c. 822 CE; *Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- `[minor]` **Place-binding** — certain yōkai manifest as or are inseparably fused with a specific natural feature (mountain pass, river, ancient tree); the location becomes an extension of their body; destroying the site harms or disperses the yōkai

## Strengths

- Not subject to physical laws governing mortal creatures — can pass through barriers, appear without warning, and vanish without trace
- Heightened awareness of their bound territory; cannot easily be ambushed or surprised within it

## Characteristics

- Extreme morphological diversity — no single body plan; spans animal-like, humanoid, fully formless, and object-like forms
- Behavioral alignment ranges across the full spectrum from benevolent and protective to mischievous and deceptive to lethal
- Liminal ontology — occupy the boundary between kami (divine spirit) and monster; the same being may be venerated as a local kami in one village and feared as a yōkai in the next; the distinction depends on local worship and context (*Nihon Shoki*, 720 CE; *Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- Nocturnal tendency — many are most active at night; the *hyakki yagyō* (百鬼夜行, "night parade of one hundred demons") is a mass procession of yōkai through settlements on certain nights (*Hyakki Yagyō Emaki*, 16th century; *Taiheiki*, c. 1370)

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Susceptibility to Buddhist ritual — sutra recitation, exorcism (*kaji-kitō*), and purification rites can pacify, bind, or destroy yōkai; the most consistent counter-force across the entire corpus (*Nihon Ryōiki*, c. 822 CE; *Konjaku Monogatarishū*, c. 1120 CE)
- Ontological instability — in multiple tales, correctly naming or identifying a yōkai breaks its power over a victim or forces it to flee
- Dependency on local belief — a yōkai's presence and potency are tied to the traditions and fears of the local community; it weakens where it is unknown or disbelieved

## Modifiers

None attested at the category level in primary sources. Individual yōkai may carry specific curses, divine punishments, or compulsions — see roster entries.

## Allies / Rivals / Enemies

| Faction | Standing | Notes | Source |
|---------|----------|-------|--------|
| Kami | Rival | Many yōkai are fallen or unworshipped kami; certain beings (kappa, inugami) are treated as local kami in some communities and as yōkai in others — the boundary is fluid and contested, not inherently adversarial | *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE); *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |
| Buddhist clergy | Enemy | Priests recite sutras and perform exorcisms to pacify or destroy yōkai; the most consistent human counter-force across the Heian–Kamakura literary record | *Nihon Ryōiki* (c. 822 CE); *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |
| Onmyōji (yin-yang masters) | Enemy | Court diviners specialising in detecting and banishing supernatural entities; primary secular opposition to dangerous yōkai in the capital | *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |
| Humans | Rival | Relationship varies by type — some yōkai harass, deceive, possess, or prey on humans; others coexist peacefully, offer guidance, or actively protect a household or locality | *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE); *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |

## Encounter conditions

| Mode | Trigger / Condition | Protocol | Source |
|------|---------------------|----------|--------|
| Hyakki yagyō (night parade) | The procession passes through a settlement on certain nights — any mortal who witnesses it risks being taken, cursed, or driven mad | Stay indoors with doors barricaded; recite Buddhist sutras; those who look directly upon the parade may vanish or die before dawn | *Hyakki Yagyō Emaki* (16th century); *Taiheiki* (c. 1370) |
| Possession (tsuki-mono) | A yōkai spirit latches onto a spiritually weakened, grieving, or ritually exposed person | Summon a Buddhist priest or onmyōji; ritual exorcism (*kaji-kitō*) drives the spirit out; the host may recover or perish depending on how long possession has lasted | *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE); *Nihon Ryōiki* (c. 822 CE) |
| Shapeshifter unmasking | A bakemono in human guise attempts to deceive or lure a victim | Bright light, fire, or Buddhist invocation forces the yōkai to reveal its true form; once revealed it typically flees or attacks | *Konjaku Monogatarishū* (c. 1120 CE) |

## Ecology

**Prey:** Varies by type — predatory yōkai (oni, serpent-types) hunt humans or animals for food; others subsist on spiritual energy, fear, or offerings rather than physical matter; non-predatory types do not hunt

**Predators:** Buddhist clergy and ritual (primary threat to existence, not a biological predator); onmyōji; more powerful kami opposing malevolent entities; in tale cycles, heroes who carry divine protection or blessed weapons

> **Note on sources:** Yōkai as a formal category was codified gradually. The term appears in 8th-century official chronicles as a word for anomalous phenomena, and the rich literary typology — named subtypes, individual tales, visual catalogues — develops across the Heian and Kamakura periods (794–1333). No single primary source defines the full category; this profile synthesises across texts spanning the 8th–15th centuries. The boundary between yōkai and kami is explicitly contested in the sources themselves and should be treated as a design variable rather than a settled distinction.
