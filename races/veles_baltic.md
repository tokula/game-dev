# Vėlės

> See also: [veles_baltic_roster.md](veles_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Vėlė (Lithuanian singular), Vėlės (Lithuanian plural); Latvian cognate *veļi* (plural); from Proto-Balto-Slavic *\*weliā*, from PIE *\*welh₃-* "to strike, to kill" — the root denotes those who were struck down by death; the same PIE root yields the theonym *Velnias* (making the name of the shepherd and his flock etymologically cognate), Old Icelandic *valr* ("the slain on the battlefield"), and Tocharian *wäl* ("to die"); *vėlė* applies specifically to the spirits of dead humans — not animals; a related but distinct concept is *siela* (the vital living breath that undergoes reincarnation into trees, plants, and animals, separate from the *vėlė* that departs to the afterlife realm)

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition — *dainos* (folk songs) and folk belief; Jonas Basanavičius, *Iš gyvenimo vėlių bei velnių* (1903–1904), whose title translates to "From the Life of Souls and Devils," is the primary written repository; Jan Łasicki, *De diis Samagitarum...* (1582), attests the feast of dead souls and names *Vielona* as the deity receiving offerings

**Biological Form:** Undead

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Male and Female — every deceased person becomes a vėlė, retaining the sex and general appearance of the deceased; no gender transformation at death is attested

## One-line lore summary

Every Lithuanian person who dies becomes a vėlė — a shade that retains its identity, memory, and care for the living, residing in a distant realm but returning to its family's table at the autumn feast of souls to receive offerings and be asked, at the feast's end, to leave in peace.

## Specializations

- `[minor]` Physical force from the spirit state — vėlės traveling in flocks can raise storms and tear off rooftops if their path is blocked by structures built across their traditional routes; they exert measurable physical force on the world despite being spirits

## Strengths

- Retain full individual identity after death — appearance, memory, personality, and emotional bonds to their descendants are preserved
- Ancestral guardianship — protective of their living relatives, especially dead parents over orphan children; the connection persists as long as the living honor the dead
- Travel by multiple means: flight as birds along the Milky Way (*Paukščių Takas*, "the Birds' Way"), rising with fire-smoke, riding horses through the sky, or traveling westward by boat through the waters
- Can communicate with the living during the Vėlinės feast period — be addressed by name, receive food and drink

## Characteristics

- Universal category — every deceased human becomes a vėlė; this is not a supernatural minority but the universal post-death state for the human species within Lithuanian cosmology
- Semi-present rather than purely incorporeal: they can receive and consume material offerings; they occupy a place at the table during the feast; they travel in perceptible flocks
- The *siela* (vital breath) and the *vėlė* (departing shade) are distinct: the siela undergoes reincarnation into the natural world; the vėlė goes to a distinct afterlife realm
- Two afterlife destinations are attested: (1) **Dausos** — a celestial realm on a high mountain between two rivers, with perpetual daylight and golden apple trees, governed by Vėjopatis (Lord of the Wind); (2) a chthonic realm under **Velnias** — a pastoral landscape where souls browse like livestock in lush meadows; both traditions are included as they appear to represent parallel cosmological strata rather than contradictions
- Communal, organized existence in the afterlife — described as dwelling on a sandy hill with houses, chambers, tables, and linen coverings, mirroring burial-mound architecture
- Retain the sex and appearance from life; travel in flocks along their own paths through the world
- Return to the living world primarily at **Vėlinės** (the autumn feast of souls, originally *Ilgės*, now aligned with November 1–2); secondary return occasions include Good Friday and Kūčios (Christmas Eve), when the boundary between the living and the dead was held to dissolve
- *Klajojanti vėlė* ("wandering soul") — a named condition for a vėlė that has no living descendants, was improperly buried, or has an unresolved grievance; not a separate sub-type but a state of displacement that can produce harm in the household or territory

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Dependent on the living for peace — vėlės who receive no offerings or recognition become restless; the *klajojanti vėlė* condition arises from neglect or improper burial and can produce harm
- Bound to the Vėlinės compact — their annual return is conditional on being invited and properly hosted; if the compact breaks (no feast, no offerings), the relationship between the living and the dead deteriorates
- Path-bound — vėlės travel established routes; structures built across those routes create friction that can manifest as storms or physical damage, a consequence of obstruction rather than an act of will

## Modifiers

- Governed by Velnias — *Velnias is the chthonic deity and etymological kin (both names share PIE *\*welh₃-*); he is described as a shepherd of the dead, with vėlės browsing as his flock in lush meadows; origin: Lithuanian folk tradition; Basanavičius (1903–1904)*
- Admitted to Dausos by Vėjopatis — *good souls are guided by the psychopomp Auštaras to the celestial Dausos; Vėjopatis (Lord of the Wind) governs the gate; harmful souls are blown into oblivion; origin: Lithuanian folk tradition, compiled from dainos*
- Bound by the Vėlinės compact — *the living must host the dead at the autumn feast — preparing food, pouring libations (first sip/bite to the spirits, poured under the table or into the floor corner), lighting fires and candles, calling the dead by name, and at the feast's end explicitly asking the vėlės to leave without trampling the newly sown grain; failure to maintain the compact enables the klajojanti vėlė condition; origin: Lithuanian folk practice*

## Ecology

**Prey:** N/A — divine/immortal beings; vėlės receive material offerings (food, drink, fire) during the feast but do not hunt or prey on the living

**Predators:** N/A — divine/immortal beings; the primary threat to a vėlė's peace is neglect by the living (triggering the *klajojanti* condition) rather than a natural predator

> **Note on sources:** The two afterlife destinations (Dausos and Velnias's chthonic realm) may represent different cosmological strata within Lithuanian belief — a sky/earth split, a pre-Christian/syncretic split, or regional variation — rather than a single coherent system. Both are documented and both are included as Characteristics reflecting the full range of the tradition. Łasicki (1582) names *Vielona* as the deity receiving dead-soul offerings at the feast of souls, indicating a divine overseer whose role partially overlaps with Velnias's; that figure was not included in this session's batch. The *siela / vėlė* distinction (two post-death destinations for two aspects of a person) is a recurring feature of Lithuanian soul-belief attested in folk song tradition.
