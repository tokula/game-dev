# Daimones — Greek Roster

> See also: [daimon_greek.md](daimon_greek.md)

## Command hierarchy

No formal hierarchy governs daimones as a class. Zeus dispatches them collectively as guardians and ministers of divine order (*Works and Days*, Hesiod); individual daimones of specific functions (Nemesis, Tyche, Hypnos, Thanatos) operate autonomously within their domains. A personal daimon serves its assigned mortal across a lifetime without formal oversight from other daimones. The Olympians are the ultimate superior authority, but daimones act without a chain of command among themselves.

**Source priority:** Primary sources (Hesiod's *Works and Days* and *Theogony*; Plato's *Symposium*, *Apology*, *Republic*, and *Phaedo*) are authoritative.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

**Agathos Daimon**
- `[Characteristic]` Protective serpent form — depicted specifically as a serpent in Agathodaemon worship imagery; the serpent form is intrinsic to its household guardian function, not a transformation; households kept small serpents as Agathos Daimon manifestations
- `[Characteristic]` Symposium protocol — the first unmixed cup of wine at any Greek symposium was poured to the Agathos Daimon before any other libation; this made it a ritual participant in all formal banqueting

**Phaethon**
- `[Modifier]` Transformed into a daimon after death — Hesiod specifies that Zeus elevated Phaethon to the status of an incorporeal divine daimon, guardian of Aphrodite's shrine; this is an unusual posthumous transformation, not his natural state — *Theogony*, Hesiod, ll. 986–990

**The personal daimon of Socrates**
- `[Characteristic]` Advisory function only — Plato's *Apology* is emphatic that Socrates's daimonion only warns (negative signal: "do not do this") and never commands positive action; it functions as a brake, not a guide
- `[Specialization-minor]` Immune to disguise — the daimonion cannot be deceived about what would harm Socrates; it operates at a level of insight beyond rational argument or persuasion
