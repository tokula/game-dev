# Yūrei — East Asian Roster

> See also: [yurei_east_asian.md](yurei_east_asian.md)

## Command hierarchy

No collective hierarchy governs yūrei — each is autonomous, bound by its own unresolved purpose. There is no leadership, organisation, or communication between individual yūrei in the primary sources.

The closest analogue to hierarchy is the historical **goryō cult**, in which politically powerful onryō were formally ranked and appeased in order of their perceived danger:
- **Goryō** (noble onryō) — implicitly ranked by the scale of disaster attributed to them; those capable of city-wide destruction (Prince Sawara, Prince Nagaya) received the most elaborate ritual appeasement
- **San O-Yūrei** (三大幽霊, "three great yūrei") — a cultural ranking of the three most famous literary yūrei: Oiwa, Okiku, and Otsuyu; this is a theatrical tradition, not an in-world hierarchy

**Source priority:** Primary sources (folklore, mythology, religious texts, historical accounts) are the authoritative origin. Games and fiction derive from them. Always populate `— Primary sources` first and most completely.

**Section naming rule:** `— Primary sources` is used here because yūrei have a real-world folkloric and literary base.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

Only traits that deviate meaningfully from the yūrei category baseline are listed.

**Oiwa**
- `[Specialization-major]` Persistent curse beyond death — her onryō wrath extends to anyone involved in staging her story in theater or film; numerous accounts (post-dating the original play) describe misfortune befalling productions that skip the traditional appeasement ritual at Myōgyōji temple before casting; this curse-extension pattern is unique among the San O-Yūrei
- `[Fault]` Disfigurement visible in manifestation — her appearance carries the specific disfigurement caused by the poison (facial drooping, one eye clouded); unlike most yūrei whose appearance is the generic white-kimono archetype, Oiwa is recognisable by her injury

**Okiku**
- `[Fault]` Compulsive incompletion — permanently unable to count past nine; cannot complete her count, cannot move on; this incompletion is the mechanism of her haunting, not a weakness exploitable for exorcism; the ninth number is the structural lock that keeps her bound
- `[Specialization-minor]` Location binding — so tightly fused to the well at Himeji Castle that she cannot manifest elsewhere; her domain of haunting is a single fixed point

**Otsuyu**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Perfect living appearance — appears fully human to her lover with no indication of her ghost nature; only a third party who knows she is dead can perceive the truth; the peony lantern is the only outward signal
- `[Modifier]` Binding through love, not rage — unlike onryō, her emotional compulsion is longing rather than anger; she does not harm her target but her continued visits drain his life force regardless of intent — *Otogi Bōko* (1666)
- `[Fault]` Love as trap — her lover's growing dependence on her nocturnal visits makes him unwilling to accept that she is dead; this mutual emotional bond accelerates his decline; the haunt cannot resolve because neither party wants it to

**Prince Nagaya**
- `[Specialization-major]` Imperial family targeting — his onryō wrath focused specifically on members of the imperial family rather than the general population; the precision of a goryō's vengeance differs from the undifferentiated disaster of less focused onryō
- `[Characteristic]` Earliest documented onryō cult — his death in 729 CE and subsequent haunting established the template for goryō appeasement that later cases (Prince Sawara, Sugawara no Michizane) followed

**Prince Sawara**
- `[Specialization-signature]` City-scale disaster — his spirit produced epidemics, natural disasters, and sustained civic harm severe enough that Emperor Kanmu relocated the capital to escape it; the only onryō in the Shoku Nihongi whose influence forced a geopolitical decision
- `[Modifier]` Posthumous imperial enshrinement — granted the title Sudō Tennō (Emperor Sudō) after death to placate his spirit; enshrinement elevated him from a vengeful onryō to a kami, ending the active haunting — *Shoku Nihongi* (797 CE)
