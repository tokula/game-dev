# Satyrs — Greek Roster

> See also: [satyr_greek.md](satyr_greek.md)

## Command hierarchy

Dionysus is the divine patron and ruler of the satyr *thiasos* (divine company); all satyrs are ultimately subject to his authority. Silenus functions as the elder of the satyr kind and as Dionysus's tutor, giving him informal senior status above other satyrs but no formal command authority. Among individual satyrs, there is no hierarchy — they are a pack of equals driven by the same appetites.

**Source priority:** The fragmentary *Catalogue of Women* (attributed to Hesiod), Herodotus's *Histories*, and Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* are the primary sources. Ovid's *Metamorphoses* supplements with individual myths.

## Individual distinctions — Primary sources

**Ampelus**
- `[Specialization-signature]` Posthumous transformation into the grapevine — Dionysus, grief-stricken at Ampelus's death, transforms him into the first grapevine; every cluster of grapes is, in this tradition, an aspect of Ampelus; Dionysus's gift of wine to humanity is inseparable from this loss; *Dionysiaca*, Nonnus, Bks 10–12
- `[Characteristic]` Recklessness as the defining trait — Ampelus's death results from boasting; he claims superiority over a wild animal; the episode exemplifies the satyr fault of hubris in concentrated form

**Marsyas**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Musical skill exceeding mortal range — Marsyas is skilled enough on the aulos to challenge and nearly match Apollo, the god of music; no mortal or satyr in primary sources comes as close to equalling an Olympian in their domain; his skill is genuine, not mere delusion
- `[Fault]` Hubris leading to catastrophic punishment — the challenge itself constitutes hubris (Athena had discarded the instrument as demeaning; Marsyas elevates himself by taking it up and claiming it rivals Apollo's lyre); the punishment — flayed alive — is proportionate to the audacity by Olympian standards
- `[Modifier]` Flayed alive by Apollo — the punishment is permanent (Marsyas is dead); his blood becomes the River Marsyas in Phrygia; *Metamorphoses*, Ovid, Bk 6.382–400; *Bibliotheca*, Apollodorus, 1.4.2

**Silenus**
- `[Specialization-minor]` Hidden wisdom — Silenus possesses profound knowledge of existence that is inaccessible while he is intoxicated; when captured and sobered, he can reveal deep truths; Herodotus records that when captured by Midas he said the best fate for mortals would be not to have been born, and the second-best to die as quickly as possible (*Histories* Bk 8.138)
- `[Characteristic]` Permanent intoxication as baseline state — Silenus is never fully sober under normal conditions; his drunkenness is not occasional but constitutive; he requires wine to function; he rides a donkey because he cannot walk steadily; this is the defining comic and pathetic characteristic of the elder satyr
- `[Fault]` Captureable via wine — Silenus's appetite for wine is so absolute that it overrides all instincts for self-preservation; Midas exploits this systematically and successfully; the capture protocol is effective precisely because Silenus cannot resist
