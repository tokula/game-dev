# Draugar

> See also: [draugar_norse_roster.md](draugar_norse_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Draugar (Old Norse: plural; singular *draugr*); etymology debated — possibly from a root cognate with Gothic *driugan* "to perform military service / endure" or related to a root meaning "phantom, ghost." Early Eddic usage carries a sense of ghostly warrior-apparition; Icelandic saga usage (the dominant record) refers specifically to a corporeal revenant. Also called *aptrgangr* ("again-walker," the general term for revenants) or *haugbúi* ("mound-dweller," a subtype that does not leave its burial mound).

**Source Layer:** Norse

**Primary origin:** Norse mythology and Icelandic saga tradition — primarily *Eyrbyggja saga*, *Grettis saga*, *Laxdæla saga*, *Hervarar saga ok Heiðreks* (all c. 13th century); rarer earlier usage in the Poetic Edda

**Biological Form:** Undead

**Gender(s):** Male and Female — both sexes are attested as draugar in saga literature; male warriors are the most commonly featured, but female revenants appear in several sagas

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Warrior culture

## One-line lore summary

Corporeal Norse undead revenants who rise from their burial mounds with amplified strength and malice, haunting the living as a consequence of improper burial, violent death, or sheer refusal to remain dead.

## Specializations

- `[major]` **Mass and size increase** — Draugar can increase their physical bulk and mass at will; some grow to the size of an ox or a great flayed bull, making wrestling or restraint increasingly dangerous as the fight continues
- `[major]` **Supernatural strength** — Their physical strength greatly exceeds what they possessed in life, and in many accounts exceeds that of the strongest living men (Grettir, among the strongest men in saga tradition, barely overcomes Glámr)
- `[minor]` **Shape-change** — Some draugar can take animal forms; Víga-Hrappr of *Laxdæla saga* is attested in seal form
- `[minor]` **Darkness-casting** — Some draugar create temporary darkness during daylight hours, giving themselves a tactical advantage and causing terror

## Strengths

- Retain all combat skills and warrior knowledge carried into death
- Conventional weapons are ineffective against many draugar; iron and fire are the reliable counters
- Can leave the burial mound and roam actively across a wide territory
- Some draugar can cause weather disturbances, livestock death, madness in farmhands, and blight across their local area
- Curses: at least one attested draugr (Glámr) can lay a dying curse on its killer

## Characteristics

- Corpse-blue (*hel-blár*, "death-blue") or corpse-pale (*nár-fölr*, "corpse-pale") skin
- Foul stench of decay
- Fully corporeal — not a ghost or spirit; they can be grappled, restrained, and physically destroyed
- Haunt their burial mound (*haugr*), farmstead, or territory; often guard treasure buried with them
- Driven by malice toward the living; no documented cases of peaceful or benign draugar in primary sources

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Decapitation followed by burning of the corpse is the definitive destruction method documented across multiple sagas
- Sufficiently strong warriors can physically wrestle a draugr into submission (Grettir vs. Glámr is the canonical example), though not necessarily destroy it
- Folk preventive measures attested in sources: iron nails through the coffin lid, scissors placed on the chest, shoes tied on incorrectly (*helskor* removed) — these prevent rising but do not destroy an active draugr
- The strongest draugar are typically overcome by individuals with exceptional physical strength, not by conventional combat

## Modifiers

- **Curse of improper death / ritual failure** — A person who dies violently without proper burial rites, is denied a grave, or possesses such overwhelming will or malice that they refuse the normal transition to the afterlife, rises as a draugr. This is an imposed condition arising from ritual failure or individual character — it is not inherent to all dead, only to those who fail the conditions of proper passage. — *Eyrbyggja saga; Grettis saga; general saga tradition*

## Allies / Rivals / Enemies

| Faction | Standing | Notes | Source |
|---------|----------|-------|--------|
| Humans (Midgard) | Enemy | Draugar prey on local humans and livestock; they are an active, persistent threat to the surrounding community | *Eyrbyggja saga*; *Grettis saga* |

## Ecology

**Prey:** Humans and livestock — draugar kill and sometimes devour both; some draugar ride the roofs of farmhouses at night, killing inhabitants; cattle death and human madness are documented secondary effects of a powerful draugr's presence

**Predators:** Human warriors with exceptional strength; fire (the definitive destruction method). No supernatural predator is documented.

## Game depictions (variants from the source mythology)

### The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim (Bethesda, 2011)
Draugr are depicted as armoured undead Nord warriors inhabiting ancient burial crypts, serving as standard enemy types. The corporeal undead identity is retained but stripped of the highly individual, territorial nature documented in primary sources — saga draugar are unique and powerful; Skyrim draugr are a common enemy category.

---

> **Note on sources:** The distinction between *draugr* (active revenant who roams) and *haugbúi* (mound-dweller who stays in its grave) is drawn by modern scholars from saga evidence but is not always cleanly maintained in primary texts. The Poetic Edda uses *draugr* in a more poetic, less technical sense than the sagas. The richest and most detailed primary record is the Icelandic family sagas, where draugar function as a recurring narrative device for exploring themes of violent death, improper burial, and community disruption.
