# Nereids

> See also: [nereid_greek_roster.md](nereid_greek_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Νηρηΐδες (*Nērhéïdes*); singular Νηρηΐς (*Nērhéïs*); daughters of Νηρεύς (*Nēreus*), the old man of the sea; Nereus's name may derive from the root *nar-* or *ner-* (water, liquid), cognate with a Proto-Indo-European root for "submerging."

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology — Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE) names all fifty Nereids (ll. 243–262); Homer's *Iliad* (c. 8th century BCE) portrays them in collective lament; Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* and Hyginus's *Fabulae* expand individual myths.

**Biological Form:** Humanoid (semi-divine)

**Entity type:** Race/Species

**Gender(s):** Female

**Common archetype/role in fiction:** Nature spirits, Divine servants

## One-line lore summary

The fifty sea-daughters of the truthful old man Nereus — divine maidens of the Mediterranean who attend Poseidon, calm storms for sailors, and appear in collective mourning when great heroes fall.

## Specializations

- `[minor]` **Calm-water command** — Nereids can ease or still the seas within their domain; Hesiod calls three of them (Cymodoce, Cymatolege, and Amphitrite) out specifically as powers who still the waves on the sea (*Theogony* ll. 253–254).
- `[minor]` **Shapeshifting** — several Nereids, most notably Thetis, can transform into other forms to evade capture; Thetis shifted through multiple shapes (fire, water, beast) when Peleus seized her; attested in Pindar's *Odes*.

## Strengths

- Prophetic lineage: their father Nereus is "the Old Man of the Sea," famed for truthful prophecy and wisdom; this truth-telling quality is shared by his daughters, particularly Nemertes ("the unerring one"), who is explicitly noted as having "her father's mind" (*Theogony* l. 262).
- Access to Poseidon's court and the deepest sea trenches; uniquely positioned among sea spirits.
- Collective mourning as divine acknowledgment: when the Nereids rise together to lament (*Iliad* Bk 18.35–51), it signals the cosmic significance of a mortal's fate.

## Characteristics

- Exactly fifty in number per Hesiod's *Theogony* (ll. 243–262); combined lists from Apollodorus, Hyginus, and Homer yield more than fifty names due to variant traditions across sources.
- Daughters of Nereus (a Protogenos-descended sea deity) and the Oceanid Doris; granddaughters of Oceanus and Tethys on their mother's side.
- Appear as beautiful sea-maidens; often depicted riding sea creatures (dolphins, hippocampi) or accompanying Poseidon's procession.
- Attend Poseidon and Amphitrite; Amphitrite herself is one of the fifty and became Poseidon's queen.

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Bound to the Mediterranean sea domain; their power is attenuated far from open saltwater.
- Their divine nature does not protect them from grief: the Nereids collectively weep for Achilles and Patroclus in the *Iliad*, suggesting they experience full emotional pain.

## Modifiers

None attested as a class. Thetis's forced marriage to Peleus and the prophecy about her son are individual modifiers — see roster.

> **Note on sources:** Hesiod's *Theogony* (ll. 243–262) provides the canonical list of fifty Nereids. Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* (1.2.7) provides a largely parallel list. Homer's *Iliad* (Bk 18.35–51) lists names of Nereids who came to mourn with Thetis — this list partially overlaps with Hesiod's but includes variant names. No ancient source fully reconciles all the names; where variant names appear in different sources, both are noted in the roster.
