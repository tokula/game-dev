# Žemyna

> See also: [zemyna_baltic_roster.md](zemyna_baltic_roster.md)

**Native name / Etymology:** Žemyna (Lithuanian) — from *žemė* ("earth"); diminutive forms: *Žemynėlė*, *Žemelė* (both express tenderness and intimacy — "dear earth," "little earth"); from Proto-Indo-European *dʰéǵʰōm* ("earth"); cognate with Thracian *Zemele* and Greek *Semelē* (Σεμέλη, mother of Dionysus in Greek myth), suggesting a shared ancient earth-mother archetype across the Indo-European world

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition — first documented in Jan Łasicki, *De diis Samagitarum caeterorumque Sarmatarum et falsorum Christianorum* (1582); described in Mikalojus Daukša, *Catechismus* (1595); the ritual *žemyneliauti* (pouring the first sip of every drink onto the earth as libation) documented by Matthäus Prätorius, *Deliciae Prussicae* (1690); Daniel Klein, *Grammatica Litvanica* (1653); Lithuanian *dainos* and agricultural ritual prayers, oral tradition compiled 19th century

**Biological Form:** Spirit (immanent earth deity — not a being who governs the earth from outside it, but a being who is the earth)

**Gender(s):** Female

## One-line lore summary

The most ancient and most intimate of Lithuanian deities — the living earth herself — honored at every meal with a poured libation, guardian of the household threshold, and the final recipient of every person who dies and returns to her soil.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Immanent divine presence in all Lithuanian earth — Žemyna does not govern the earth; she is the earth; every field, threshold stone, and burial mound is simultaneously her body and her domain; this is not a claim to territory but a statement of identity
- `[major]` Agricultural yield as direct expression of her will — harvest abundance and failure are not consequences she causes; they are her direct response, without mechanism or intermediary; her favor or disfavor manifests as the earth's fertility itself

## Strengths

- Universally accessible — the simplest libation (first sip of beer, first bite of bread poured onto or broken on the earth) establishes direct contact; she requires no specialist, no temple, no trained priest
- Protects the household threshold — the earth at the doorway is her domain; nothing passes over it without her awareness
- Sacred grass snakes (*žalčiai*, singular *žaltys*) are her living avatars within the household; a home with a resident žaltys is under her direct protection; killing a žaltys brings severe misfortune
- Receives the dead into her body — she is the final recipient of every burial; what Velnias governs within, she is the substance of

## Characteristics

- Immanent, not transcendent — she does not appear in human form or speak in visions; she is experienced through the earth itself, through growth and soil and the weight of ground underfoot
- The ritual *žemyneliauti* (pouring the first sip of every drink onto the earth as libation) was practiced at every household meal from before recorded history through at least the 17th century; Prätorius (1690) documents it persisting in folk practice despite active Christian suppression
- Associated with the household threshold, the hearth-foundation, and burial mounds — all liminal points where the living world meets the earth's interior
- Connected to Velnias through the complementary earth/underworld relationship: Žemyna is the surface and substance of the earth; Velnias governs the realm within it; the two are structurally inseparable
- The earliest Lithuanian deity attested in written records (Łasicki, 1582) and almost certainly one of the oldest in Lithuanian folk memory

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Non-interventionist by nature — Žemyna does not act directly in the world; her blessing or displeasure manifests only through natural processes (fertility, soil health, growth); she cannot be called upon for immediate miraculous intervention
- Desecration of her earth or the killing of her sacred snakes (*žalčiai*) constitutes a severe ritual offense, but her response is natural consequence (failed harvests, household misfortune) rather than active retribution

> **Note on sources:** Žemyna is the best-attested of the eight beings in this Baltic chthonic batch — first recorded by Łasicki in 1582 and documented continuously through the 17th century by Klein and Prätorius. The *žemyneliauti* ritual (pouring libation to the earth) is one of the most consistently described pre-Christian Lithuanian practices across multiple independent sources. Post-Christianization suppression is documented by Prätorius himself, who records that the practice persisted despite church prohibition. The Greek cognate *Semelē* (mother of Dionysus) and Thracian *Zemele* suggest Žemyna's archetype is one of the oldest recoverable strata of Indo-European religion, though the specific Lithuanian characterization is culturally distinct.
