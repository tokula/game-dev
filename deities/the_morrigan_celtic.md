# The Morrígan

**Native name / Etymology:** Morrígan (Old Irish) — most likely "Phantom Queen": the first element derives from a Proto-Indo-European root of terror and monstrousness (cognate with Old English *maere*, source of "nightmare"); *rígan* means "queen". A secondary reading "Great Queen" (Middle Irish *mór* "great" + *rígan*) was popularised in the medieval period but is considered a secondary folk etymology by most modern scholars.

**Source Layer:** Celtic

**Primary origin:** Irish mythology — Ulster Cycle (*Táin Bó Cúailnge*, oldest MS: *Lebor na hUidhre*, c. 1106) and Mythological Cycle (*Cath Maige Tuired*, c. 9th–10th century)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Singular Deity

**Gender(s):** Female — consistently gendered female in all primary sources; shape-shifts into animal forms but her identity and social standing remain female throughout

## One-line lore summary

An Irish goddess of war, sovereignty, and fate who appears at battles as a shape-shifting crow, prophesies death and victory, and confers or withdraws the right of kings to rule.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` Shape-shifting across radically different life forms — attested forms in primary sources: crow/raven, eel, wolf, heifer, and old woman; shifts are deliberate and tied to specific narrative goals
- `[major]` Battle prophecy — foretells death, doom, or victory before and during conflict; announces battle outcomes aloud from elevated vantage points; her presence at a battle is itself an omen

## Strengths

- Sovereignty conferral — can bless or withhold the legitimacy of a king's rule over territory, cattle, and people
- Divine standing — equal authority to the Dagda among the Tuatha Dé Danann; not subordinate

## Characteristics

- Triple-goddess aspect — sometimes manifests as or is collectively named with Badb and Macha (and occasionally Nemain) as the *Morrígna*; the exact composition varies by source (see Sources note)
- Liminal affinity — associated with Samhain (boundary between seasons) and fords (boundaries between territories); her encounters with Cú Chulainn occur at liminal spaces
- Sovereignty-linked to the land — her relationship with the Dagda in *Cath Maige Tuired* frames her as a source of territorial blessing as well as war

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Vulnerable in animal form — in the *Táin Bó Cúailnge*, Cú Chulainn wounds her in each of the three animal forms she takes (eel, wolf, heifer); the wounds persist until she is unknowingly healed by him while disguised as an old woman

## Modifiers

None attested — her attributes are intrinsic to her nature.

> **Note on sources:** The composition of the *Morrígna* triple goddess varies by source. *Cath Maige Tuired* groups Badb, Macha, and the Morrígan (the last sometimes equated with Anu); other texts substitute Nemain for one of the three. Modern scholarship increasingly treats these figures as aspects or epithets of a single divine being rather than fully distinct entities. No single primary text resolves the composition definitively.
