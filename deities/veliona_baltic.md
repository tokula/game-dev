# Veliona

**Native name / Etymology:** Veliona (Lithuanian); derived from *vėlė* ("soul of the deceased") + feminine nominal suffix. The town of Veliuona on the Nemunas River is named for her and was once a centre of her veneration. Root shares PIE *welh₃-* ("death") with Velnias and the Slavic Veles.

**Source Layer:** Baltic (Lithuanian)

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition and mythology, attested in *De diis Samagitarum* by Jan Łasicki (c. 1582, published 1615)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Singular Deity

**Gender(s):** Female

## One-line lore summary

Lithuanian goddess of the dead and protector of souls, caretaker of all vėlės (spirits of the deceased) and guardian of the subterranean realm, venerated at the Vėlinės festival each November.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Guardianship of souls** — sole divine caretaker of all vėlės (souls of the deceased) after death; her authority over the dead is protective rather than punitive
- `[major]` **Dominion over four elements** — secondary sources attribute to her command over water, air, fire, and earth, reflecting her role as an underworld sovereign connected to all natural forces
- `[major]` **Keeper of subterranean treasures** — guardian of wealth and hidden valuables buried in the earth

## Strengths

- Fertility influence — extends protection to the land, domestic animals, and livestock, particularly those killed in the hunt or on the farm
- Receives direct worship through food offerings crumbled over fields and buried in the ground at Vėlinės

## Characteristics

- Venerated on **Vėlinės** (the festival of souls, synchronized with the Christian All Souls' Day on November 2nd), when offerings are made to honour the dead
- The town of **Veliuona** (Lithuania) preserves her name; a church was later built over her former sanctuary site there
- Closely associated with Velnias — some folk sources describe them as consorts, though no primary text confirms a formal relationship

## Faults / Weaknesses

None specifically attested in primary sources.

## Modifiers

- **Christian calendar overlay** — the Vėlinės festival was absorbed into the Catholic All Souls' Day (November 2nd), gradually shifting veneration of Veliona into a generalised remembrance of the dead — *imposed by Christian missionary and ecclesiastical practice over pre-Christian Baltic tradition*
