# Velnias

**Native name / Etymology:** Velnias (Lithuanian); syncoped form of *vẽlinas*, derived from *vėlė* ("ghost / soul of the dead") + PIE root *welh₃-* ("death"). Cognates: Latvian *velns*; Old Icelandic *valr* ("the slain in battle"); Proto-Germanic *Walhall-*. The name means roughly "lord of the vėlės" (souls of the dead).

**Source Layer:** Baltic (Lithuanian)

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition and mythology, earliest substantial written attestation in *De diis Samagitarum* by Jan Łasicki (c. 1582, published 1615)

**Biological Form:** Spirit

**Entity type:** Singular Deity

**Gender(s):** Male

## One-line lore summary

The Lithuanian god of the underworld and the dead, lord of the souls of the deceased (*vėlės*), and chief chthonic counterpart to the sky-and-thunder god Perkūnas.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Lord of the Underworld** — governs the realm of the dead and all vėlės (souls) within it; no living being or lesser spirit exercises authority over this domain
- `[major]` **Command of vėlės** — can marshal and lead armies of dead souls, manifesting as whirlwinds and storms crossing the sky

## Strengths

- Chthonic authority — unquestioned dominion over subterranean and underworld spaces
- Patron of the oath-bond between the living and the dead — folk tradition invokes him as guarantor of promises made to deceased kin

## Characteristics

- Described in folk sources as one-eyed, echoing other chthonic or wisdom-linked figures across Indo-European myth
- Associated with the *vėlės* collectively — the souls of all the dead, not only the heroic or honoured
- Name equated with *Piktis* ("the evil/dark one") by Sirvydas (1629), reflecting Christian reinterpretation; the original function is chthonic, not malevolent

## Faults / Weaknesses

- Persistently opposed by Perkūnas (the storm/sky god) — thunder and lightning are his natural antagonists; Perkūnas actively pursues and strikes at Velnias across the sky
- After Christianization, demoted wholesale to a devil figure, losing his status as a major deity in popular memory

## Modifiers

- **Christianization** — conflated with the devil and evil personified by Christian missionaries and scribes; the equation survives in Sirvydas's *Dictionarium* (1629) — *imposed by 16th–17th century Catholic missionary doctrine over pre-Christian Baltic tradition*

> **Note on sources:** Łasicki (1582/1615) did not speak Lithuanian and relied on second-hand accounts, so his deity list should be treated as indicative rather than authoritative. Sirvydas's equation of Velnias with "Piktis" (devil) is a Christian-filtered reading, not a description of the pre-Christian deity. Attributes commonly assigned to Velnias in modern summaries (cattle, snakes, wealth) are well-attested for the cognate Latvian *Velns* and Slavic *Veles*, but their direct attestation in Lithuanian primary sources is limited — treat these as probable by cognate inference rather than confirmed primary-source fact.
