# Nyx

**Native name / Etymology:** Νύξ (*Nyx*); from Proto-Greek *nukts; PIE *nókʷts meaning "night"; cognate with Latin *nox*, Sanskrit *nakṣ-*, and English "night" — one of the most widely attested and undisputed Proto-Indo-European roots.

**Source Layer:** Greek

**Primary origin:** Greek mythology — Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE) is the primary source for her cosmogonic role and progeny; Homer's *Iliad* (c. 8th century BCE) attests her unique status as the one being Zeus himself would not openly antagonise.

**Biological Form:** Elemental (divine)

**Entity type:** Primordial Being

**Gender(s):** Female

## One-line lore summary

The primordial goddess who IS the night itself — one of the first entities to emerge from Chaos, mother of death, sleep, fate, and strife — and the only being in all of Greek myth that even Zeus will not openly defy.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **IS night** — Nyx does not govern or personify the night; she is its substance. Her presence in the cosmos is the night itself. No training, power, or divine status could replicate this ontological identity.
- `[signature]` **Beyond Zeus's authority** — the *Iliad* (Bk 14.258–261) records that when Zeus wished to punish Hypnos for aiding Hera against him, he refrained when Hypnos fled to Nyx: Homer states Zeus "feared to do anything displeasing to swift Nyx." She is the only entity in surviving Greek myth whose presence inspires restraint in Zeus through fear — not respect or love, but fear.

## Characteristics

- Emerged from Chaos alongside Erebus (Darkness) as one of the first-order entities of the cosmos (*Theogony* ll. 123–125).
- With Erebus, produced Aether (bright upper sky) and Hemera (Day) — the first generative light-pair, born as opposites of their parents (*Theogony* ll. 124–125).
- Alone, without a father, produced a vast brood of personified forces — primarily dark or morally ambiguous: Moros (Doom), Ker (Destruction), Thanatos (Death), Hypnos (Sleep), the Oneiroi (Dreams), Momos (Blame), Oizys (Pain), the Hesperides, the Moirai (Fates), the Keres, Nemesis (Retribution), Apate (Deceit), Philotes (Affection), Geras (Old Age), and Eris (Strife) (*Theogony* ll. 211–225).
- Described in the Homeric tradition as dwelling at the edge of the cosmos, at the boundary threshold where Hemera (Day) and Nyx pass each other — never sharing the same space simultaneously (*Theogony* ll. 744–757).
