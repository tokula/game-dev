# Žemyna

**Native name / Etymology:** Žemyna (Lithuanian) — from *žemė* ("earth"); diminutive and ritual address forms: *Žemynėlė*, *Žemelė* (both express tenderness — "dear earth," "little earth"); from Proto-Indo-European *\*dʰéǵʰōm* ("earth"); cognate with Thracian *Zemele* and Greek *Semelē* (Σεμέλη, mother of Dionysus in Greek myth), reflecting a shared Indo-European earth-mother archetype; related Lithuanian male figures share the root — *Žemėpatis* ("Earth Spouse") and *Žemininkas* (deity of cattle and agriculture)

**Source Layer:** Baltic

**Primary origin:** Lithuanian oral tradition — first documented in Jan Łasicki, *De diis Samagitarum caeterorumque Sarmatarum et falsorum Christianorum* (1582); described in Mikalojus Daukša, *Catechismus* (1595); the ritual *žemyneliauti* (pouring the first sip of every drink onto the earth) documented by Matthäus Prätorius, *Deliciae Prussicae* (c. 1690); Daniel Klein, *Grammatica Litvanica* (1653); Lithuanian *dainos* (folk songs) and agricultural ritual prayers, oral tradition compiled 19th century

**Biological Form:** Spirit (immanent earth deity — not a being who governs the earth from outside it, but a being who *is* the earth)

**Entity type:** Singular Deity

**Gender(s):** Female

## One-line lore summary

The most ancient and most intimate of Lithuanian deities — the living earth herself — honored at every meal with a poured libation, guardian of the household threshold, and the final destination of every person who dies and returns to her soil.

## Specializations

- `[signature]` **Ontological immanence** — Žemyna does not *govern* the earth; she *constitutes* it. Her presence is indistinguishable from the physical substance of earth itself: every field, stone, and household threshold is simultaneously part of her being. No other deity, mortal, or ritual force can replicate this ontological identification — other gods govern domains; Žemyna *is* her domain.
- `[major]` **Living-dead cycle** — She is simultaneously the origin of all living things (all life is nourished by and born from the earth) and the receptacle of all the dead (all burials are returns to her; she rules and guards the departed). In Baltic theology these are not two roles — they are one: she is the substance through which life passes in both directions.

## Strengths

- Sustains all life on earth simultaneously — human, plant, and animal are nourished by her without distinction or mediation
- Omnipresent at the household threshold — honored at every meal, every agricultural act, and every rite of passage, making her the most consistently ritually active deity in Lithuanian domestic religion

## Characteristics

- Her sacred grass snakes (*žalčiai*) dwell in household gardens as living expressions of her presence; they are not commanded by her but recognized as her avatars — harming them invites misfortune
- Addressed exclusively through affectionate diminutives in ritual speech (*Žemynėlė*, *Žemelė*); no formal or distant mode of address is attested, reflecting the intimate, familial character of her cult
- The first sip of every drink at any celebration or agricultural event is poured on the ground (*žemyneliauti*) as libation; bones and scraps of sacrificial animals are burned or buried as offering; bread baked from the prior harvest's last crops is buried in the field before new sowing

## Faults / Weaknesses

- No directed agency — she does not intervene, appear, or speak; she receives ritual and offering but does not respond through visible apparition, audible speech, or active intervention in mortal affairs; she cannot be summoned, dispatched, or prevailed upon to act in any direction
- No attested role in divine conflicts — while Perkūnas fights Velnias and Laima sets fates, Žemyna has no documented participation in the decisions or battles of the divine pantheon; she is the ground upon which cosmic events occur, not a participant in them

| Praamžius | Ally | Alternative consort in some Lithuanian sources — the same earth-sky dyad expressed with the sky-father (*Praamžius*) rather than the thunder-god; the two attestations represent regional or temporal variants of the same motif | Lithuanian oral tradition (secondary summary: Wikipedia "Žemyna", Role—Family) |

> **Note on Velnias:** Velnias governs the underworld realm *within* the earth that Žemyna constitutes. Their relationship is structural complementarity rather than alliance, rivalry, or conflict — he rules the interior realm; she *is* the substance enclosing it. No primary source documents cooperation, competition, or enmity between them directly.
